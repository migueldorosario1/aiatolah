# 2 Tech Stocks I Would Buy Before SpaceX

SpaceX captures the imagination like few companies in history. Reusable rockets, Starlink internet, and a mission to colonize Mars make it the ultimate story stock. Yet for all its achievements, SpaceX remains private, its equity locked behind accredited-investor walls. The moment someone asks where to invest today for the next decade of compounding, the honest answer must be: look at the publicly traded companies building the scaffolding of the next industrial revolution. That leads directly to two semiconductor giants whose shares are available to anyone with a brokerage account. If forced to choose between holding an unreachable SpaceX stake and deploying capital into real, liquid innovation leaders, I would buy these two names first.

Nvidia needs little introduction. The company has transformed from a gaming graphics specialist into the central nervous system of artificial intelligence. Its H100 and upcoming Blackwell GPUs are the de facto brains inside the data centers trained by every major AI lab. What makes Nvidia a superior investment to the promise of SpaceX is not just market position, but compounding economics. The CUDA software ecosystem creates a wide moat that competitors find nearly impossible to cross. Enterprises training large language models do not simply buy hardware; they invest in an entire parallel computing platform that integrates libraries, developer tools, and optimization frameworks built over nearly two decades. This software advantage locks in customers and generates recurring revenue far beyond a one-time chip sale. Meanwhile, demand from cloud hyperscalers, sovereign AI projects, and enterprise inference workloads shows no signs of slowing. Nvidia’s gross margins regularly exceed 70 percent, translating operating leverage into explosive free cash flow. That cash is being poured into research, acquisitions, and stock buybacks. Unlike SpaceX, which burns capital on capital-intensive launches and satellite constellations with uncertain long-term returns, Nvidia is a money-printing machine that dominates the defining technology of our era. For an investor seeking exposure to the AI megatrend with actual ownership and liquidity, Nvidia is the obvious choice over any private space venture.

The second stock I would buy before SpaceX is Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, or TSMC. If Nvidia designs the AI brain, TSMC etches the silicon neurons that make it work. TSMC is the world’s most advanced contract chip manufacturer, the only company capable of producing cutting-edge nodes at scale. Every Nvidia H100, every Apple A-series processor, every AMD Epyc CPU, and a growing share of automotive and IoT chips flow through TSMC’s fabs. This monopoly on advanced process technology is arguably even more unassailable than SpaceX’s lead in reusable rockets. Building a leading-edge foundry requires hundreds of billions of dollars, decades of accumulated process knowledge, and a delicate geopolitical balance that TSMC has mastered. The company’s investment cycle is monumental, with CapEx in 2024 alone exceeding the GDP of some small nations, yet it remains fabulously profitable. TSMC’s pricing power has strengthened because the world has woken up to the strategic nature of chip supply chains. Governments from Washington to Berlin to Tokyo are throwing subsidies to secure domestic fab capacity, but the process gap means TSMC will remain the critical supplier for years. As AI models balloon in size and complexity, demand for 3nm and soon 2nm chips will surge, directly benefiting TSMC’s revenue per wafer. The stock carries geopolitical risk tied to China-Taiwan tensions, but that risk is arguably priced in and mitigated by the company’s global diversification into Arizona, Japan, and Germany. When I compare this to SpaceX’s dependence on a single charismatic leader and a volatile launch market, TSMC emerges as a far more durable and predictable wealth compounder.

The temptation to romanticize SpaceX is understandable. Its engineering feats are genuine breakthroughs. But investment is about buying future cash flows at a discount, not marveling at past launches. Both Nvidia and TSMC sit at the center of the AI value chain, generating enormous returns on invested capital while remaining accessible to everyday investors. SpaceX may one day go public, but until then, those focused on building wealth through transparent, liquid, and dominant franchises should start with these two semiconductor titans. They are the picks I would make every single time before chasing a private rocket dream.
