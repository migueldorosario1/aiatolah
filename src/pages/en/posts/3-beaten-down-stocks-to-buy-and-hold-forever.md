3 Beaten-Down Stocks to Buy and Hold Forever

The artificial intelligence revolution is nowhere near its final chapter, yet the stocks powering it have been treated like yesterday’s news. A combination of tariff anxiety, a shift away from high-momentum names, and unrealistic short-term expectations have carved deep discounts in the world’s most essential AI infrastructure companies. For investors with a time horizon measured in decades, not days, this is precisely the kind of dislocation that builds generational wealth. Three beaten-down semiconductor and AI enablers stand out as forever holds: <strong>Nvidia</strong>, <strong>Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company</strong>, and <strong>Broadcom</strong>.

The pullback in <strong>Nvidia</strong> (NVDA) is the most glaring opportunity. After scaling above $150 per share, the stock has retraced sharply, briefly dipping below $90. Market chatter points to fears that cheaper Chinese models like DeepSeek could reduce demand for high-end chips. The reality is the opposite. Efficiency gains in inference lower the total cost of deploying AI, widening the addressable market and pulling forward demand for the most powerful training clusters. Nvidia’s CUDA moat remains impenetrable, the Blackwell ramp is just beginning, and sovereign AI buildouts from the Middle East to Asia are adding a completely new layer of hyperscale spending. A forward price-to-earnings ratio that has compressed into the low 20s for a company delivering triple-digit data center growth is a gift that calm-minded investors can hold forever.

No Nvidia chip reaches a data center without <strong>Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company</strong> (TSM). The foundry’s shares have been beaten down by perennial geopolitical fear, but the business itself has never been stronger. TSMC’s monopoly on advanced packaging (CoWoS) and its accelerating roadmap to 2-nanometer and beyond make it the single most critical node in the global AI supply chain. Chinese large language models like Kimi and Qwen may be trained on locally designed accelerators, but the most ambitious ones still rely on Nvidia GPUs — and all of them, directly or indirectly, trace back to TSMC’s fabs. With capacity sold out well into 2027 and a capex plan that ensures technological leadership for a decade, the recent dip in the ADR represents a permanent addition to a long-term portfolio.

For exposure that spans beyond GPUs, <strong>Broadcom</strong> (AVGO) provides the pick-and-shovel networking backbone that makes giant AI clusters possible. Shares retreated from over $250 to the $180 range as the market worried about a cyclical slowdown, but AI-related revenue inside Broadcom is anything but cyclical. The company’s custom ASIC programs for hyperscalers, combined with its dominance in Ethernet switches and PCIe connectivity, will only grow as AI training clusters scale to 100,000 accelerators or more. The acquisition of VMware also gives Broadcom a sticky software annuity that balances the hardware cycle. A dividend that has compounded for years and a management team obsessed with capital returns make this a stock to tuck away and forget.

China’s homegrown model builders — DeepSeek, Kimi, Qwen — are not a threat to this trio. They are an accelerator. Every incremental AI workload, regardless of origin, requires more compute, more networking, and more advanced manufacturing. The recent punishment handed out to these shares is based on a narrative that confuses short-term volatility with structural decline. Permanent capital can be deployed here with confidence. When the fear fades, these three stocks will once again be priced as the irreplaceable pillars of the intelligence age.
