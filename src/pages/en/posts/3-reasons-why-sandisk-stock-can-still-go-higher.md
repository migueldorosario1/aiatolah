3 Reasons Why Sandisk Stock Can Still Go Higher

The relentless expansion of artificial intelligence infrastructure, propelled in large part by Nvidia’s data center dominance, is silently supercharging demand for high-performance storage. Sandisk, a pure-play flash memory leader after its spin-off from Western Digital, finds itself at the center of this tectonic shift. While the market fixates on GPU shortages, it often overlooks a critical fact: massive AI workloads require equally massive, low-latency storage to feed data-hungry models. Here are three reasons why Sandisk shares are positioned for further gains.

First, the AI-driven data center capex cycle has entered a new phase where storage is no longer an afterthought. Nvidia’s latest GPU architectures, such as the H200 and Blackwell platforms, significantly increase data throughput, requiring all-flash arrays that can keep pace. Sandisk’s enterprise-grade SSDs, built with its proprietary NAND technology, are becoming a go-to choice for cloud providers and AI labs constructing the next wave of training and inference clusters. As Nvidia ships more GPUs, the attached flash storage footprint expands almost proportionally. This multiplier effect—often underestimated—gives Sandisk a durable tailwind that outlasts a single product cycle.

Second, Sandisk’s product roadmap is beating the market to higher capacity nodes at a time when power efficiency and density matter most. The company is aggressively ramping its BiCS8 218-layer QLC NAND, delivering better bit cost scaling than many competitors. For hyperscale customers, this translates into denser, more energy-efficient storage racks. In an environment where data center power budgets are strained to the limit, Sandisk’s ability to offer more terabytes per watt provides a distinct competitive moat. Preliminary adoption signals from memory module partners indicate that the new node is seeing faster qualification cycles than its predecessor, a sign that customers are eager to upgrade and are willing to pay a premium for the efficiency gain.

Third, Sandisk’s valuation still does not fully reflect the structural shift in its revenue mix toward enterprise and hyperscale segments. For years, the company’s top line was heavily tethered to volatile consumer demand for client SSDs and removable storage. The post-split Sandisk is leaner, more focused, and generates a growing share of its income from sticky, long-cycle data center contracts. Consensus estimates have yet to fully price in the margin expansion that will come as enterprise revenue surpasses 50% of the mix. Meanwhile, the stock trades at a discount to the sector on both forward price-to-earnings and enterprise-value-to-EBITDA multiples, even as its earnings per share revisions trend upward. As quarterly results increasingly reflect the AI storage boom linked to Nvidia’s ecosystem, this discount is unlikely to persist.

Skeptics will point to memory cycle risks—and NAND prices do have a history of sharp swings. However, the current upcycle is distinct in one critical way: supply discipline among top producers has held, and demand is being driven by a secular investment wave, not a mere inventory restock. With AI model parameters ballooning and multimodal data requiring ever more back-end storage, the floor for NAND consumption is rising. Sandisk, riding the coattails of Nvidia’s data center revolution, has a path to re-rate higher as the market connects the dots between GPUs and the storage silicon that makes them truly productive.
