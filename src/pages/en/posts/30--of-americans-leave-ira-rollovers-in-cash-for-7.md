Thirty percent of Americans who roll over a 401(k) into an IRA leave the entire balance sitting in cash for a full seven years, according to data tracking default settlement behavior. The inertia is staggering — and the financial consequences are brutal. When you let a rollover check land in a brokerage sweep account and never lift a finger to invest it, you are not playing it safe. You are actively destroying your future purchasing power while inflation and missed compounding do the heavy lifting for you.

The most immediate cost is the deceptively gentle drag of near‑zero yield. Cash sweep vehicles at major IRA custodians have paid less than half a percent annually for most of the past decade. On a $100,000 rollover, seven years of stagnant cash might add perhaps $800 in total interest. Over the same period, cumulative inflation has eroded the real value of that cash by more than 20 percent. What felt like $100,000 in 2018 now buys only about $79,000 worth of goods and services. That alone is a punishing loss, but it is the foregone investment returns that turn a mistake into a lifelong regret.

Consider the simple alternative of a plain S&P 500 index fund. From January 2018 through the end of 2024, the total return of the S&P 500 — reinvesting all dividends — roughly doubled. That same $100,000 rollover, invested in nothing more exotic than a low‑cost equity fund, would have grown to around $200,000. By staying in cash, the typical inactive saver surrendered over $100,000 of potential wealth without taking any meaningful risk. That forfeited amount alone is larger than the median 401(k) balance of Americans in their fifties.

And if you want to understand the true cost of missed opportunity, look no further than the poster child of the artificial intelligence boom: Nvidia. At the start of 2018, a $100,000 IRA rollover could have purchased shares of Nvidia at a split‑adjusted price near $1.50. Seven years later, with Nvidia trading around $120, that same stake would be worth approximately $8 million. The 30 percent of Americans who left their rollovers in cash did not just miss out on a comfortable retirement increment; they missed the kind of wealth creation that defines generations. Most people would have been perfectly fine capturing a fraction of that gain through an index fund that holds Nvidia as a top position, yet even that required the basic step of moving money out of cash.

The behavioral root of this cost is a toxic mix of fear and decision paralysis. Many people mistakenly believe that leaving money uninvested is a neutral, safe harbor while they figure things out. In reality, it is an aggressive bet against growth, one that compounds uncertainty into a guaranteed shortfall. Seven years is not a brief pause; it is a full market cycle. Employers and plan sponsors have made automatic enrollment and default target‑date funds the standard inside 401(k) plans for exactly this reason. But the moment a rollover hits an IRA, those protective defaults vanish. The individual is suddenly the fiduciary, and the default becomes cash — often permanently.

Brokerage and advisory platforms bear part of the blame. Sweep account interest rates remain low even in a rising‑rate environment because many firms treat client cash as a profit center. Savvier investors manually move idle cash into high‑yield money market funds or short‑term Treasuries, but the people most likely to leave money untouched are the least likely to navigate those menus. Regulators and consumer advocates have pushed for better nudges, including default into low‑risk diversified portfolios rather than raw cash, but the industry’s resistance is fierce because sweeps are lucrative.

The takeaway for any saver who has rolled over a retirement account is brutally simple: if you have not logged in and explicitly invested that balance, you are probably part of the 30 percent. The clock is ticking on the second most expensive mistake you can make in your IRA, right behind not contributing at all. Even a conservative allocation to a balanced fund would have turned idle cash into tens of thousands of additional dollars over the past seven years. Next time someone tells you they are waiting for a better moment to invest their rollover, point them to the Nvidia chart. The best moment was the day the check cleared. The second‑best moment is today, before another seven years of nothing slip away.
