Apple’s Worldwide Developers Conference is expected to be a defining moment for the company’s artificial intelligence ambitions, with a rejuvenated Siri and system-wide generative AI features taking center stage. While the narrative will be about how Apple finally delivers a coherent AI experience across its devices, the underlying hardware implications are just as significant — not least for Nvidia, the company that has come to personify the AI boom.

For more than a year, Nvidia has ridden an extraordinary wave of demand for its data center GPUs, driven by a frantic race among hyperscalers and startups to build and train large language models. Every major AI initiative, from OpenAI’s GPT-4 to Google’s Gemini, has been powered by thousands of Nvidia H100 accelerators. Apple’s sudden and intense focus on generative AI — long after rivals like Google and Microsoft had already embedded the technology into their products — reinforces just how universal this arms race has become. When the world’s most valuable company by market capitalisation feels compelled to overhaul its core operating systems around AI, it sends a clear signal that spending on AI infrastructure will keep accelerating. Nvidia, as the dominant supplier of that infrastructure, remains the immediate winner.

Yet Apple’s approach also contains the seeds of a longer-term challenge to Nvidia’s hegemony. Apple is not the typical Nvidia customer. For its on-device machine learning, it leans heavily on its own Neural Engine, a component of its custom M-series and A-series chips. More importantly, recent reports suggest that Apple is developing its own large-scale inference systems using clusters of M2 Ultra chips housed in data centers, a move aimed at running the more demanding AI tasks while maintaining its privacy-first narrative. If Apple can successfully serve millions of Siri and AI requests without buying a single H100, it would demonstrate that custom silicon can scale beyond consumer devices into the cloud, potentially inspiring other tech giants to accelerate their own chip programmes.

That scenario is not an immediate threat. Apple’s server efforts remain nascent, and the sheer magnitude of model training still overwhelmingly favours Nvidia’s parallel processing architecture and its CUDA software ecosystem, which is deeply entrenched in the AI research community. Furthermore, the broader surge in AI demand that Apple’s WWDC announcements will help catalyse — more apps, more agents, more generative features across every industry — guarantees that Nvidia’s order books will remain full for the foreseeable future. Even if Apple sidesteps Nvidia for its own services, the millions of developers now racing to embed AI into iOS apps will ultimately rely on cloud providers that are Nvidia’s best customers.

What Apple’s WWDC ultimately underscores is that the AI revolution has reached a stage where vertically integrated players are moving to own the full stack, from the chip to the end-user experience. Nvidia’s near-monopoly on training hardware is safe for now, but as inference workloads become the dominant volume driver — and as companies like Apple prove that custom silicon can handle those workloads with compelling economics — the landscape could slowly fragment. For Nvidia, the message from Apple’s developer conference is dual: the AI pie is growing even larger than anticipated, but the battle over who bakes it is far from over.
