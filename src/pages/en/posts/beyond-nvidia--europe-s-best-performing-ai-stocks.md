Beyond Nvidia: Europe’s best-performing AI stocks of 2026

Nvidia has been the undisputed face of the artificial intelligence revolution. Its GPUs power the world’s most advanced models, and the stock’s meteoric rise defined the early years of the AI investment cycle. But as 2026 unfolded, the market’s gaze shifted. While Nvidia’s shares showed signs of maturity—consolidating rather than sprinting—savvy investors turned to a quieter, less obvious AI story: Europe. A continent often dismissed as a tech laggard has quietly produced some of the year’s best-performing AI equities, driven by industrial AI, sovereign capacity, and a deep bench of semiconductor equipment and enterprise software champions.

Leading the charge is ASML Holding. The Dutch lithography giant remains the only company on Earth that can build the machines needed to produce the most advanced chips—including those Nvidia depends on. In 2026, ASML’s order book swelled as governments and chipmakers raced to build domestic fabrication capacity. Its next-generation high-NA EUV systems became the bottleneck for AI hardware expansion. The stock surged by double digits, reflecting not just earnings growth but a strategic repricing: the world finally understood that there is no AI future without ASML.

Germany’s SAP was another standout. The enterprise software titan completed its cloud transformation just as AI copilots and business agents moved from pilot to production. SAP’s Joule assistant, deeply embedded into S/4HANA and SuccessFactors, drove a wave of customer upgrades. The company’s massive data moat—sitting at the heart of global supply chains and finance—allowed it to train domain-specific models that rivals could not replicate. In 2026, SAP became the go-to AI platform for the Fortune 500, pushing the stock to record highs and establishing it as Europe’s definitive software answer to Microsoft.

In the semiconductor space, Infineon Technologies and STMicroelectronics both posted strong gains. Infineon’s power chips, already essential for data center energy management, found a new growth engine in AI-driven robotics and autonomous industrial systems. STMicro’s partnerships in edge AI—especially in automotive and smart sensors—catalysed a re-rating as the market began pricing in the full scope of decentralized AI inference. These were not speculative plays; they were profitable, cash-rich firms benefiting from real, expanding AI use cases.

The United Kingdom delivered its own AI champion in RELX. Long viewed as a steady publishing and analytics conglomerate, RELX’s Legal & Professional division built one of the most sophisticated AI-driven legal research tools, rapidly adopted by law firms and corporate counsel globally. Its Exhibitions and Risk divisions also harnessed machine learning to optimize operations. The market rewarded this pivot with a valuation multiple expansion that many had reserved exclusively for pure-play AI names.

Siemens AG rounded out the list, proving that industrial AI is not just a buzzword. Its Digital Industries unit embedded AI into factory automation software, digital twins, and predictive maintenance. In 2026, Siemens became synonymous with next-generation manufacturing, executing a global rollout of AI-optimized production lines that cut costs and carbon footprints simultaneously. The stock’s steady climb outpaced many US tech giants, as investors recognized the defensive, real-world revenue streams behind its AI narrative.

What united these performances was a common thread: they all solved real, often unglamorous problems. While speculative AI chips and chatbot startups burned cash, Europe’s leaders supplied the picks and shovels, the mission-critical software, and the physical infrastructure that the AI era requires. The rally also reflected a geopolitical tailwind. European Union chip acts, renewed defense spending, and a push for sovereign AI capabilities funneled billions into regional companies, insulating them from US-China trade frictions that occasionally rattled Nvidia.

As 2026 closes, the lesson is clear. Nvidia remains indispensable, but betting on AI is no longer a one-stock story. Europe’s AI ecosystem has matured, producing a diversified basket of winners that delivered returns benchmark investors can no longer ignore. The best-performing AI stocks of the year were not chasing the next big language model—they were building the foundation on which the entire AI edifice stands.
