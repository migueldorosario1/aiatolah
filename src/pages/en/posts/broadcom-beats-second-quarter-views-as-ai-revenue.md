Broadcom’s second-quarter revenue beat and a doubling of AI-related sales underscore an accelerating expansion of the semiconductor opportunity beyond GPUs. While Nvidia still commands the headlines and the lion’s share of AI compute spend, Broadcom’s results signal that the infrastructure buildout is leaking into networking, custom ASICs and data center interconnects, three domains where Broadcom is formidable. For Nvidia, this is less a direct competitive blow than a confirmation that its own addressable market is not a zero-sum game. Broadcom’s AI revenue, driven by hyperscaler demand for high-bandwidth switching and routing silicon, reveals that the AI data center is becoming a rich stack, with multiple beneficiaries. That dispersion of value could eventually challenge the narrative that Nvidia will capture an outsized portion of every AI dollar. Still, Nvidia retains a full-stack advantage: its CUDA ecosystem, integrated systems like DGX and forthcoming Spectrum-X networking bind customers in ways that no single component supplier replicates. Broadcom thrives by enabling the AI buildout without controlling the platform lock-in. The near-term risk for Nvidia is not that Broadcom steals GPU share but that the market starts rewarding infrastructure breadth as a strategic hedge. If custom chips from Broadcom’s ASIC partnerships reach parity on cost-per-inference, cloud titans may tilt capital away from merchant GPUs. For now, both companies are riding the same capex tsunami; the real test will be whose moat deepens as the cycle matures.
