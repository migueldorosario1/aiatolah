Cathie Wood Is Buying Nvidia and Dumping This AI Chip Giant

The most anticipated daily disclosure in tech investing did not disappoint on January 27. While panicked traders were vaporizing nearly $600 billion from Nvidia’s market cap in a single session, Cathie Wood’s ARK Invest was methodically adding to its position. Her team snapped up shares of the GPU leader just as the market was pricing in an existential threat from DeepSeek. At the very same moment, ARK was exiting another AI semiconductor titan almost as aggressively. The trade tells a story not about short-term fear, but about a layered bet on the architecture of artificial intelligence.

The buying speaks to a conviction that has been missing from ARK’s relationship with Nvidia for years. Wood famously trimmed her Nvidia stake long before the generative AI supercycle ignited, a decision that became a case study in leaving the next big thing too early. Returning to the name at the point of peak maximum pain signals a recognition that the DeepSeek selloff was an overreaction built on a misunderstanding. The market interpreted the Chinese lab’s cost-efficient training as a signal that the world needs fewer GPUs. ARK appears to see the opposite. As models become cheaper to train, the inference workload — the repeated, energy-intensive act of running those models — explodes. And inference is where Nvidia’s newly ramped Blackwell architecture is purpose-built to dominate. Wood’s move suggests a reading that the DeepSeek moment is not an end to the GPU spending cycle, but a catalyst for its next, more distributed phase.

The counterparty to that Nvidia purchase was Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company — the giant being dumped. ARK sold a significant chunk of TSMC shares on the same day, a decision that at first glance seems to contradict the bullish Nvidia thesis. TSMC fabricates every leading-edge AI accelerator, including Nvidia’s own chips. But the sale reveals a strategic hierarchy of risk. TSMC, for all its monopoly-like power, sits at the apex of concentrated geopolitical hazard. The company remains a focal point of the escalating technology cold war between Washington and Beijing, facing persistent headline risk from the Strait of Taiwan. The DeepSeek episode itself added a new layer: it demonstrated that American export controls on advanced silicon have not prevented Chinese labs from achieving competitive breakthroughs. While this does not reduce TSMC’s short-term revenue, it muddies the narrative that the foundry’s position is unassailable forever. ARK’s shift from TSMC to Nvidia is less a verdict on fabrication and more a preference for the asset sitting closer to the innovation edge — software-defined, more asset-light, and already weathering the worst of the margin compression fears.

There is also a valuation calculus. Nvidia, even after a breathtaking two-year run, was suddenly trading at a multiple that felt dislocated from its forward earnings power once the panic dust settled. TSMC, by contrast, had run up dramatically on the back of its own AI capacity narrative and was priced for perfection. For a fund built on high-conviction, long-duration disruption ideas, swapping a richly valued geopolitical wildcard for a temporarily impaired compute platform monopoly is the kind of asymmetric wager that defines ARK’s philosophy.

What the twin trades ultimately signal is a maturing perspective on the AI stack. Cathie Wood is not simply chasing a falling knife or liquidating a winner capriciously. She is positioning for a world where the bottlenecks shift. When DeepSeek and its ilk make training cost collapse, the chokepoint moves from the ability to build models to the ability to serve them to billions of users at latency humans accept. That world needs Nvidia’s networking muscle, its CUDA ecosystem, and its inference-optimized silicon more than it needs another wafer of uncontracted logic. By buying the panic in Nvidia and funding it with the calm in TSMC, ARK has drawn a line in the sand between the infrastructure that builds AI and the infrastructure that propagates it. Whether that line proves prescient or premature will be one of the defining investment calls of the post-DeepSeek era.
