Cerebras Systems has vaulted into the center of the AI infrastructure conversation not with a software release or a paper, but with a rapid succession of commercial pacts that signal a maturation no longer reserved for Nvidia’s captive ecosystem. The deals with Amazon Web Services and OpenAI, though distinct in structure, place a hard question before the market: what is a viable alternative to the GPU monopoly worth when it secures both the largest cloud provider and the most influential AI lab as customers?

For a company that has historically been measured by its engineering bravado — the wafer-scale engine, the CS-2 and CS-3 systems — the partnership with AWS represents a shift toward mass accessibility. By making Cerebras instances available through the cloud, the company can now tap demand from enterprises that need extreme inference speed or scalable training without purchasing dedicated hardware. The OpenAI deal is even more significant, even if details remain thin. OpenAI is the ultimate reference account, and a collaboration, whether focused on inference inference workloads or research on sparse training, instantly rewires the perception of Cerebras from outsider to insider. Both partnerships suggest the market is beginning to price a world where AI compute is not a one-vendor game.

This inevitably throws Nvidia’s valuation into relief, not because Cerebras is a near-term threat to the H100 installed base, but because it forces investors to model more than one architecture winning large-scale workloads. Nvidia’s premium has long been anchored to the assumption that the full stack — CUDA, networking, software libraries — is unassailable. Cerebras does not need to replicate that stack; it only needs to prove its architecture is superior for a subset of problems, notably those with massive memory requirements or highly sparse computations. The Amazon and OpenAI stamps of approval chip away at the narrative of absolute lock-in.

From a valuation lens, Cerebras is rumored to be eyeing an IPO at a multibillion-dollar figure, and these partnerships provide the substance to justify it. The company’s revenue has been modest relative to Nvidia’s, but the total addressable market for dedicated AI inference is expanding so fast that even a niche challenger can command serious multiples. The AWS deal opens a recurring revenue channel; the OpenAI deal opens the possibility of influencing the next generation of frontier models. Both are the kinds of anchors that late-stage investors and public markets pay for.

Yet the analysis cannot ignore the asymmetries. Amazon manufactures its own chips with Trainium and Inferentia, and OpenAI remains fully entangled with Microsoft Azure’s GPU clusters. Cerebras’s partnerships must be read as complementary rather than exclusive. The risk is that Cerebras becomes a useful bargaining chip for customers negotiating prices with Nvidia, rather than a structurally embedded part of the supply chain. The company’s long-term valuation depends on executing deeply integrated co-design, not just providing an accelerator for a handful of tasks.

For Nvidia, the immediate damage is less financial than psychological. The attention on Cerebras cracks the illusion that no alternative can even earn a trial. Should that trial convert to production, the market may begin to apply a conglomerate discount to Nvidia’s data center segment, trimming the stratospheric multiples that have come to define the stock. It is a slow-moving erosion, not a cliff, but it is now visible on the horizon.

The partnerships between Cerebras and cloud and model-scale entities crystallize a phase change in the semiconductor renaissance that Nvidia ignited. They are a reminder that leading-edge AI hardware is no longer a monoculture. Valuation models will need to accommodate this, and Nvidia’s defensive posture just became a fraction more costly.
