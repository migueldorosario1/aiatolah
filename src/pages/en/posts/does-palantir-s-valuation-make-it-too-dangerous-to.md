Does Palantir's Valuation Make It Too Dangerous to Buy?

A specter now haunts the artificial intelligence trade: the ghost of dot-com era excess. A growing chorus of analysts warns that the furious rally in AI-linked stocks has pushed certain names into territory where rational valuation models no longer apply. Palantir Technologies, the data analytics firm turned AI platform play, sits squarely in the crosshairs of this debate. When the question is anchored to Nvidia — the undisputed gold standard of AI execution — Palantir’s premium appears not just ambitious but potentially reckless for anyone buying at current levels.

Nvidia’s valuation story is unique because it is backed by an avalanche of concrete financial results. The company commands a forward price-to-earnings multiple above 40 not on hope but on triple-digit revenue growth and operating margins expanding past 60%. Every quarter, it converts breathtaking demand for H100 and Blackwell GPUs into torrents of free cash flow that make the multiple compress fast. Investors who buy Nvidia today are paying for a growth rate that, while decelerating from its peak, remains tethered to a visible, multi-year investment cycle by hyperscalers who have no choice but to build out AI infrastructure.

Palantir does not enjoy that same foundation. The company has undeniably transformed itself from a niche government contractor into a more commercial-facing AI operating system, riding the generative AI wave with its Artificial Intelligence Platform, or AIP. Revenue is growing, but at a pace — roughly 20% year over year — that would be considered pedestrian next to Nvidia’s. Its operating margins have improved markedly, and it has strung together profitable quarters. Yet the stock trades at a forward earnings multiple north of 80 and an enterprise value-to-sales ratio approaching 20, figures that imply a growth trajectory more befitting a hypergrowth semiconductor titan than a software and services provider.

This is where the danger crystallizes. Nvidia’s valuation multiple has actually declined over the past year even as the stock soared, because earnings grew faster than the share price. Palantir’s multiple, by contrast, has expanded purely on narrative — a bet that AIP will become the default operating layer for enterprise AI. That narrative is compelling. The company’s boot camps convert skeptical executives into paying customers with a speed that recalls the early days of cloud computing. But software adoption curves are inherently slower and more competitive than hardware supply chains. Microsoft, Salesforce, and a legion of startups are throwing immense resources at the same problem. Palantir’s moat, built on complex data integration and ontology, is real but not invulnerable.

The risk-reward calculus becomes stark when you overlay the macro backdrop. AI infrastructure spending, the engine driving Nvidia’s reliably gigantic orders, faces questions about return on investment if killer applications beyond chatbots fail to materialize. Palantir is positioned as a beneficiary of that next phase — the application layer — but its valuation already prices in a commanding role in that future. Should enterprise AI adoption hit a cyclical pause or a competitive bottleneck, Palantir’s premium multiple would have no cushion. Nvidia, even in a pullback, would still be repriced from extraordinary profitability to merely impressive profitability. Palantir could revert to the single-digit revenue multiples of its software peers, a fall that would inflict catastrophic losses on late-stage buyers.

There is a world where Palantir justifies its price. If it captures a significant slice of the hundreds of billions expected to flow into enterprise AI over the next decade, today’s valuation might look prescient. But that outcome is uncertain and distant. Nvidia’s valuation is anchored to data center build-outs contracted and paid for in months, not years. Buying Palantir at these levels is less an investment in documented demand and more a leveraged wager on a theoretical future. For disciplined investors who have watched Nvidia compound wealth by delivering profits that match the story, Palantir’s extreme multiple without extremly rapid earnings acceleration makes it more dangerous than any AI allocation should tolerate.
