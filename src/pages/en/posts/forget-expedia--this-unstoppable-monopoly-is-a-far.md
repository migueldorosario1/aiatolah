Forget Expedia: This Unstoppable Monopoly Is a Far Smarter Bet

When Expedia went public two decades ago, it was hailed as the travel industry’s disruptor. Today, online travel agencies are a commodity, fighting over single-digit margins in a crowded field. Meanwhile, a different kind of monopoly has emerged — one that doesn’t just capture a share of a market but defines the infrastructure of the next technological epoch. That monopoly is Nvidia. For investors who understand the architecture of modern value creation, Nvidia isn’t just a semiconductor company; it is the dominant platform on which the artificial intelligence revolution is being built. And compared to Expedia’s cyclical, easy-to-replicate business, Nvidia’s moat is so deep it might as well be a canyon.

Nvidia’s power begins with its absolute dominance in AI training and inference hardware. The company didn’t simply happen upon a hot product cycle with its H100 and upcoming Blackwell GPUs. It spent over a decade cultivating the CUDA software ecosystem, a move that turned programmable graphics chips into the universal language of parallel computing. Every major AI lab, from OpenAI to DeepSeek in China, trains its models on Nvidia hardware because the entire software stack — libraries, frameworks, optimizations — is built around CUDA. This is a classic lock-in effect: researchers learn CUDA, new models are developed for CUDA, and enterprises deploy on Nvidia-powered infrastructure. Competitors like AMD or custom chips from Amazon and Google can chip away at the fringes, but they face a network effect that grows stronger with each passing day.

Consider the numbers. Nvidia’s data center revenue has surged from a few billion dollars annually to a run rate that will likely exceed $100 billion in fiscal 2025. Gross margins hover near 75%, a figure more typical of a luxury software brand than a hardware manufacturer. This margin profile is not a fluke; it reflects the pricing power of a company whose products are considered critical infrastructure. When a business cannot function without your chip, you set the terms. Expedia, by contrast, operates on margins in the low double digits at best, constantly fighting for traffic against Booking Holdings, Google Travel, and direct hotel bookings. Its competitive advantage is the travel tax it extracts from a fragmented supply chain — a tax that can be eroded by any well-funded alternative or a shift in consumer habits.

The monopoly metaphor fits Nvidia in another crucial way: its product cycle is not a simple commodity refresh. Each generation of GPU delivers a quantum leap in performance that actually expands the total addressable market. The H100 made large language models practical; the B200 and GB200 will enable real-time AI applications that were impossible before. Demand is so intense that allocation meetings among cloud providers and sovereign wealth funds resemble OPEC gatherings, only Nvidia decides who gets how much. This is not a market share battle; it is a market creator. And crucially, the barriers to entry are not merely technological but temporal. Even if a hypothetical competitor matched Nvidia’s silicon design within five years, they’d still need to replicate the software stack and developer mindshare. By then, Nvidia will have moved on.

What about China? Reports of domestic alternatives like Huawei’s Ascend chips or the efficiency breakthroughs from models such as DeepSeek have sometimes rattled Nvidia’s stock. However, those reactions misunderstand the situation. DeepSeek’s innovative training methods, for instance, were achieved on clusters of Nvidia GPUs. The frugality of Chinese AI firms does not reduce the need for compute; it makes high-performance compute even more valuable because it can be stretched further. And while export restrictions pose a risk, Nvidia has adeptly crafted compliant solutions that still dominate the alternative channels. The demand from Chinese state-backed enterprises and private giants is voracious, and no alternative ecosystem is genuinely ready for prime time. Nvidia’s monopoly extends across borders, supply constraints and all.

Investors often hunt for “the next Nvidia” in other sectors, but the real opportunity might simply be Nvidia itself, even at a $2 trillion-plus market capitalization. The company’s forward price-to-earnings multiple has been compressing as earnings explode, a pattern reminiscent of early-stage platform companies before the market fully appreciates their grip on a secular trend. Comparing it to Expedia is instructive: Expedia’s earnings are tied to the health of an already mature travel industry, vulnerable to recessions, pandemics, and shifting consumer loyalty. Nvidia’s earnings are tied to the digitization of intelligence itself — a trend that makes the internet’s expansion look like a dress rehearsal.

The real risk for Nvidia is not competition but concentration risk: a slowdown in hyperscaler capex or a macro shock that curtails AI investment temporarily. Yet the hyperscalers are in an arms race where pausing means losing the cloud and AI platform war. They will spend because they have no choice. That makes Nvidia the arms dealer in a conflict that shows no sign of ending.

For investors willing to look beyond the traditional consumer tech lottery, Nvidia offers a rare combination: monopoly economics, secular demand, and a technological advantage that looks more like a permanent dynasty than a temporary lead. Forget Expedia. The smarter bet has been staring us in the face, its chips humming away in data centers around the globe, powering the future one token at a time.
