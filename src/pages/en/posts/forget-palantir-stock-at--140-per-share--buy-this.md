Forget Palantir Stock at $140 per Share. Buy This Artificial Intelligence (AI) Chip ETF Instead

Investors who chased Palantir Technologies to $140 a share are now nursing a brutal hangover. The data-analytics darling, once bid up on AI euphoria, has tumbled roughly 45% from its February peak, erasing tens of billions in market value. The sell-off reflects a healthy recalibration: Palantir is a fine company, but a $140 price tag implied perfection that its government-weighted revenue mix and lofty multiple simply cannot deliver in a rising-rate, cost-conscious environment. If you are tempted to buy the dip, step back. The smarter AI trade today is not a single software name trading at 30 times forward sales; it is a diversified basket of semiconductor stocks — the fundamental enablers beneath every large language model.

Palantir’s problem is not its product. Its Gotham and Foundry platforms are deeply embedded in Western defense and corporate supply chains. The issue is valuation and narrative exhaustion. At $140, the stock traded at roughly 75 times trailing earnings and 30 times forward sales. Even after the correction, the multiple remains stretched relative to the growth profile. The company’s commercial acceleration is real, but it is already priced in, and a meaningful portion of recent growth came from private-sector SPAC rescues and speculative AI consulting projects that may moderate as enterprises move from trial to scaled deployment. Meanwhile, the government segment — still over half of revenue — is a slow-growth anchor that limits margin expansion.

The second risk is competition. Palantir is not an AI model maker; it integrates, orchestrates and visualizes data. That is a valuable layer, but it is not the pick-and-shovel play. As cloud hyperscalers and system integrators roll out their own AI-driven analytics, differentiation erodes. Customers may eventually prefer to consume AI insights natively through platforms like Microsoft Fabric, Databricks or even Snowflake, rather than maintain a separate, expensive ontology layer. The moat is narrower than the bull case assumes.

Contrast that with the semiconductor supply chain. Every AI query, every training run, every inference call requires computation — and the vast majority of that computation flows through accelerators designed by Nvidia, manufactured by TSMC, and supported by networking chips from Broadcom and memory from Micron. This is not a winner-take-most software market; it is an ecosystem in which multiple players compound returns as AI adoption scales. By purchasing a pure-play AI chip ETF, an investor gains exposure to Nvidia’s data-center dominance, but also to the second-order beneficiaries that Palantir shareholders miss entirely.

The VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) and iShares Semiconductor ETF (SOXX) are two vehicles that offer just such exposure. SMH is more concentrated, with a quarter of its weighting in Nvidia alone, and another 12% in TSMC. The remaining portfolio captures the entire value chain: ASML’s lithography machines, Broadcom’s custom AI ASICs, AMD’s GPU challenger, and the networking and memory names that turn a chip into a computing fabric. This portfolio trades at a forward price-to-earnings multiple below 25, roughly half what Palantir commands on an earnings basis, with revenue growth rates that easily outpace the broader market and visible demand stretching into 2026 and beyond.

Nvidia itself illustrates the difference. Its latest Blackwell architecture is sold out for the next 12 months. Hyperscalers are committing hundreds of billions in capex to AI infrastructure, and sovereign clouds are emerging as a new demand vector. Nvidia’s gross margin structurally exceeds 70%, while Palantir struggles to break 30% adjusted. The economic model is simply superior. And when you buy the ETF, you capture Nvidia’s upside while hedging against single-stock risk — a sudden export restriction, a shift in chiplet design, or a customer concentration issue. Palantir, by contrast, lives and dies on a handful of giant contracts.

There is also a macro dimension. The AI narrative is shifting from “science project” to “infrastructure buildout,” and that evolution favors hardware. Software valuations are compressing as rates stay higher for longer; hardware multiples are expanding because capex cycles are tangible and long-dated. The CHIPS Act and similar industrial policies around the world are pouring government money into semiconductor fabrication and packaging, providing a policy backstop that software pure-plays lack.

Selling Palantir here and rotating into an AI chip ETF is not a rejection of AI’s promise — it is an embrace of where the value actually accrues in this cycle. The picks and shovels are the GPUs, networking gear and memory modules that turn data into intelligence. Palantir may eventually prove its doubters wrong and carve a durable moat in enterprise AI integration. But at $140, the risk-reward was lopsided. At $60 or $70, it might be a different conversation. Even then, the core thesis remains: if you believe in AI’s transformative power, you want to own the infrastructure layer, not the application layer that competes on margin and churn. An AI chip ETF is the cleanest, most diversified way to do that — and it does not require you to bet the farm on a single, richly valued software name.
