The Shifting Sands of AI Silicon: Why Broadcom and Marvell Are Ascendant While Nvidia Plots Its Next Empire

The artificial intelligence revolution has been singularly defined by Nvidia’s GPU dominance. But as hyperscalers move from experimentation to massive deployment, a quieter tectonic shift is rewriting the semiconductor landscape. Prominent fund manager Gary Black, Managing Partner of The Future Fund, recently crystallized this transition, naming Broadcom and Marvell Technology as the "big winners" in a world increasingly leaning toward custom AI chips. His call comes just as Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, never one to cede the narrative, is already looking past GPUs, touting the arrival of the "next trillion-dollar company" in AI. The juxtaposition captures a pivotal moment: the AI chip market is fragmenting into custom silicon territory, and the spoils will not belong to a single player.

Gary Black’s thesis is rooted in plain economics. Training frontier models demands unprecedented compute density, which Nvidia’s Hopper and Blackwell architectures deliver at premium margins. But once a model is trained, the real scale lies in inference, serving billions of daily prompts to consumers and enterprises. For cloud titans like Alphabet, Amazon, and Microsoft, who deploy this at planet scale, reliance on a single, high-priced merchant GPU supplier becomes a strategic liability. Hyperscalers are not content to pay Nvidia’s 70%–80% gross margins forever. The antidote is custom silicon, application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs) designed in-house and fabricated by foundries, with design help from pioneers like Broadcom and Marvell.

Broadcom’s position is particularly enviable. It has already locked in marquee customers for its custom AI ASIC programs, most notably Google’s tensor processing units (TPUs) now in their sixth generation. Google’s TPU fleet powers everything from Search to Gemini, and Broadcom earns recurring revenue from each chip deployed in the cloud. That model is expanding to other hyperscalers. Reports suggest Broadcom is engaged with Meta and possibly ByteDance, creating a virtuous cycle of co-engineering fees, production royalties, and long-term support contracts. Marvell, though smaller, is aggressively carving its own niche, winning significant custom AI silicon contracts with Amazon, and ramping programs with Microsoft. In both cases, these are not low-margin commodity gigs; they are deeply collaborative ventures that ensure sticky, lucrative relationships for years.

This shift does not spell doom for Nvidia, but it does reshape its growth narrative. Huang understands that the market for general-purpose AI accelerators will eventually mature, and he is positioning the company to transcend hardware. His "next trillion-dollar company" is not a single product but an ecosystem: Nvidia’s DGX Cloud, Omniverse digital twins, and the CUDA software moat extend well beyond silicon. Nvidia is methodically building an enterprise AI operating system where chips are merely the entry point. Blackwell, with its giant memory bandwidth and NVLink fabric, is not just a GPU; it is a full-stack platform for the most demanding AI workloads. The company is betting that the sheer complexity of training trillion-parameter models keeps general-purpose compute indispensable, while it simultaneously captures value from software, networking, and cloud services.

Black’s commentary cuts to the core of an investor dilemma. Broadcom and Marvell represent a different kind of AI bet: they are picks-and-shovels providers to the hyperscaler spending cycle, but without the single-customer concentration risk that plagues many Nvidia watchers. If every major cloud platform eventually demands a customized chip, these two companies, with their proven design expertise and deep foundry relationships at TSMC, stand to capture a large share of the non-GPU AI market. The total addressable market for custom AI accelerators could reach tens of billions of dollars by 2027, offering a growth curve that, while not matching Nvidia’s current trajectory, provides diversification and a compelling margin profile.

The narrative is no longer one of winner-take-all. It is a bifurcation: Nvidia owns the general-purpose frontier for training and high-end inference where flexibility is paramount; Broadcom and Marvell are locking in the optimized, cost-sensitive inference volume that will dominate energy-conscious data centers. The real victor may be the overall AI buildout itself, as competition brings down the cost of deploying intelligence. For investors, the lesson is clear: a concentrated bet on a single GPU monopoly is giving way to a more nuanced, multi-pillar opportunity. Black’s focus on Broadcom and Marvell underscores that the next phase of AI infrastructure will reward the architects of bespoke silicon just as handsomely as it rewards the king of general-purpose compute. Jensen Huang’s trillion-dollar vision may well come true, but it will coexist with multiple other fortunes being minted in the custom chip foundries.
