Wall Street’s vocabulary rarely borrows from Oliver Stone these days without a knowing smirk, but when Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon casually suggested there is enough “greed” to absorb the coming wave of mega-IPOs from SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic, he was not just waxing cinematic. He was diagnosing a market condition that has left traditional valuation metrics in the dust—and at the center of this condition sits Nvidia, the silent gravitational force making those offerings thinkable in the first place.

The word “greed” is freighted, but Solomon deliberately repurposed it to describe an almost mechanical appetite. Pension funds, sovereign wealth vehicles, and retail traders chasing the artificial intelligence narrative have piled into a handful of semiconductor names with a fervor that has redefined sector concentration. Nvidia alone has ballooned into a multi-trillion-dollar entity because its GPUs are the picks and shovels of every large language model and autonomous system being built. That same hunger now sloshes beyond the public chipmakers and toward the private companies that turn Nvidia’s silicon into products with near-mythical growth stories. Solomon’s point is straightforward: there is so much dry powder and so much fear of missing out that even SpaceX—a defense-and-launch giant that does not fit neatly into an AI bucket—gets pulled into the same speculative orbit simply because its CEO is intimately tied to the tech’s public imagination.

For OpenAI and Anthropic, the Nvidia thread is even more direct. Both are among the largest consumers of H100 and B200 GPUs, and their compute bills are so staggering that only a limitless well of investor enthusiasm could justify the multi-hundred-billion-dollar valuations being whispered. In a cooler market, a firm losing money on every token generated while sprinting toward artificial general intelligence might face punishing scrutiny. Today, however, the logic loops back to Nvidia: as long as the world believes Nvidia’s revenue growth will sustain triple-digit momentum, the appetite for the companies burning those chips appears bottomless. Solomon’s comment acknowledges that the same speculative surplus that drove Nvidia past $3 trillion is now spilling into the private markets, ready to be captured by the next batch of public listings.

Yet the Goldman chief did not use the word greed entirely as a compliment. Embedded in the remark is a warning that only the naive would ignore. When a market begins to describe its own abundance in such naked terms, it often signals a late-cycle exuberance where risk is systematically mispriced. The last time a Wall Street titan invoked greed so openly, the dot-com bubble was about to burst. Back then, the enabler was Cisco; today, Nvidia occupies a similar throne, its chips the prerequisite for any credible AI narrative. If demand for those chips were to plateau—either because hyperscalers finish their buildout phase or because model efficiency reduces the need for endless GPU clusters—the valuation air pocket could extend to the very companies queuing up to go public. The IPOs of OpenAI, Anthropic, and even SpaceX would not be immune to a repricing of the underlying infrastructure upon which so much of their imagined future depends.

Solomon’s timeline, therefore, is a bet on narrative momentum outpacing the fundamentals just long enough to clear the deal pipeline. Goldman Sachs, which would stand to collect handsome fees from these listings, has every incentive to talk up the absorptive power of the market. For investors, the calculus is less cheerful: whether the greed Solomon observes turns out to be wisdom or the prelude to a hangover will be determined not by the splashy announcements of an IPO day, but by the quarterly shipments of Hopper and Blackwell GPUs. Nvidia’s earnings remain the real clearing price for risk—and if that bell tolls early, even the most fabled private names will find that greed, like fear, is a fickle underwriter.
