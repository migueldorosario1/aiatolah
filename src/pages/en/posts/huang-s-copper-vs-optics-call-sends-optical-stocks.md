Huang’s Copper-vs-Optics Call Sends Optical Stocks Surging

When Jensen Huang speaks, markets move. This week was no exception. During a technical keynote, the Nvidia CEO made a characteristically blunt assessment: copper interconnects still dominate within a rack, but optical links are inevitable for scaling beyond a few meters. The remark, almost an aside in a broader conversation about power and density, ignited a swift rally across optical component stocks. Investors read it as a signal that the optical networking era inside AI data centers is arriving faster than expected.

The market reaction was immediate and vivid. Shares of Lumentum, Coherent, and Fabrinet jumped double digits, while smaller names in the silicon photonics supply chain also caught a bid. The optics trade, which had been simmering on the promise of 800G and 1.6T transceivers for next-generation GPU clusters, suddenly had a fresh catalyst with Huang’s implicit endorsement. Analysts scrambled to reframe their models, citing Nvidia’s internal roadmap as validation that optical interconnects will move from spine-and-leaf architectures deeper into the compute fabric.

Yet a careful parsing of Huang’s words suggests the optics surge may be more about narrative than near-term earnings. He emphasized that for NVLink domain distances inside a single rack or between adjacent racks, copper cables remain the cost, power, and reliability champion. Optical is not replacing copper for short reaches anytime soon. Nvidia’s own Blackwell platform leans heavily on direct-attach copper for chip-to-chip and switch-to-NIC links. What Huang hinted at is a longer horizon: as compute scales to hundreds of thousands of GPUs, the sheer volume of copper becomes a thermal and cabling nightmare. At that point, co-packaged optics and linear drive pluggables become critical.

For the optical supply chain, this distinction matters. The bull case for many optical stocks has been built on a rapid adoption cycle inside Nvidia’s next-generation AI factories. But Huang’s comments align with a more gradual inflection. The real opportunity space is in linking GPU pods together, not replacing every copper trace with a laser. That suggests optical demand tied to Nvidia’s NVLink and InfiniBand refresh cycles, which tend to be lumpy and concentrated among a few hyperscalers. A handful of well-positioned companies will benefit, but the near-term revenue impact may not match the stock price euphoria.

Still, there is a deeper message in the optics rally that transcends the copper-versus-optics debate. Markets are increasingly treating Nvidia’s architectural preferences as the industry standard. If Huang says optics will play a larger role in scale-out fabrics, then optical connectivity becomes a must-have for any serious AI infrastructure build. This creates a halo effect that pulls forward investment not only in transceivers but also in lasers, modulators, fibers, and the entire testing ecosystem. Even if the timing is uncertain, the direction is now clear enough for institutional investors to build positions well ahead of the financial inflection.

From an Nvidia perspective, the optics enthusiasm also serves a strategic purpose. By signaling a future shift to optical, Huang reinforces the idea that scaling AI requires constant hardware innovation beyond just silicon. It keeps the ecosystem synchronized around Nvidia’s pace, encouraging suppliers to invest in capacity and technology that will ultimately lock them deeper into the Nvidia platform. Copper may win today, but optics ensures the platform stays extensible to the million-GPU clusters Nvidia envisions.

For investors, the takeaway is nuanced. The optical interconnection theme is real and will generate substantial value over the next five years. Traders who caught the immediate surge made the right call on sentiment. However, building a long-term position requires distinguishing between companies with genuine design wins inside Nvidia’s reference architectures and those simply buoyed by association. The optics food chain is long, and not everyone will feast equally. Vigilance on deployment timelines and share shifts is essential once the initial excitement fades. In the eternal Huang phrase, this is not a sprint but a marathon, and copper still has many miles left to run.
