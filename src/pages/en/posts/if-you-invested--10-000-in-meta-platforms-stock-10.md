If you had invested $10,000 in Meta Platforms stock a decade ago, your patience would have been rewarded with a healthy return, yet the true shock lies in what that same sum could have become elsewhere in the same period.

On May 22, 2015, Meta — then still called Facebook — traded around $81 per share. A $10,000 investment would have purchased roughly 123 shares. Fast forward to today, with Meta commanding roughly $580 per share, those shares would now be worth over $71,000. That marks a gain of more than 610 percent, easily outpacing the S&P 500 and rewarding investors who held through privacy scandals, congressional hearings, and a historic metaverse pivot. It is a textbook case of technological resilience.

However, for an analyst focused on the global AI and semiconductor boom, the far more arresting story is what that $10,000 could have achieved if directed toward Nvidia.

Ten years ago, Nvidia was still viewed primarily as a graphics card company serving gamers. Adjusting for subsequent stock splits, a single share could be acquired for roughly 56 cents on a split-adjusted basis. A $10,000 bet at that price would have secured nearly 18,000 shares. Today, with Nvidia’s data-center AI chips powering the infrastructure behind every major language model, those shares are worth around $1,000 each. That same $10,000 would be valued at approximately $17.8 million — a return exceeding 178,000 percent.

The contrast illuminates an essential truth about the current technology cycle. Meta’s social media dominance and algorithmic advertising generated tremendous wealth, but the exponential demand for computational intelligence has created a scale of value creation that dwarfs even the most successful platform companies. Nvidia’s processors are the backbone of the AI models that both Meta and Chinese rivals like DeepSeek, Kimi, and Qwen depend on to train and infer. While Meta built a fortress of engagement, Nvidia sold the shovels to an entire industry — and those shovels turned out to be made of gold.

For long-term investors, the lesson is not simply about picking the best stock. It is about recognizing the seismic shift from the era of social connectivity to the era of machine intelligence. The semiconductor layer of that stack, led by Nvidia and manufacturer TSMC, has accumulated an almost absurd share of the spoils. A $10,000 commitment to Meta a decade ago would have been a sensible decision that paid off; the same amount in Nvidia would have been a life-changing event. The gap between those two outcomes maps the distance between the previous technology paradigm and the one that now rules the market.
