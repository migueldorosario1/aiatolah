For more than three decades, putting money into a broad market index fund and simply waiting has been the closest thing to a layup in all of finance. From the S&P 500’s 1990 slog upward through the dot-com bust, the 2008 crisis, the pandemic shock and the AI mania of 2023 and 2024, the buy-and-hold indexer has been rewarded with a 10.5% annualized return, minimal costs and the quiet satisfaction of beating most active managers. For the average retirement account, the logic has been unassailable: own the entire market, let the winners compound, never try to time a thing.

That 33-year streak of serene automatic wealth creation is about to face its strangest test yet. Not from a recession, or a rate shock, or a geopolitical meltdown. It is coming from a rocket company. And the name that will rattle the machinery of passive investing more than any other is SpaceX — and, indirectly, the chipmaker that has become the very face of the index itself: Nvidia.

The central tension is deceptively simple. Market-cap-weighted index funds, the dominant vehicle of the passive era, are built on a brutal mathematical logic: the bigger you get, the more of you they buy. That dynamic has turned Nvidia, which added more than two trillion dollars in value in a single year, into the single largest position in the S&P 500. As of early 2025, the stock alone accounts for roughly seven percent of the entire index. Every dollar that flows into a plain-vanilla S&P 500 ETF or mutual fund automatically directs seven cents to Nvidia; every dollar that leaves yanks that much out. The passive machine has magnified Nvidia’s AI ascendancy into something almost self-reinforcing, rewarding the winner by pouring capital into it without pause.

Into this finely balanced feedback loop now steps SpaceX. The private company, valued at more than $350 billion on secondary markets, is not merely another hot tech debut. A SpaceX IPO, which CEO Elon Musk has teased could happen once Starlink achieves predictable cash flows, would almost certainly arrive as the largest initial public offering in history, instantly commanding a market cap that places it among the top ten U.S. stocks before the first day of trading is over. The passive tsunami that would follow is entirely predictable: every index fund benchmarked to the S&P 500, the total market, or large-cap growth strategies would be forced, mechanically, to buy billions of dollars of SpaceX shares within days of inclusion to maintain proper weightings. There is no judgment call. The algorithms demand it.

That is where the flawlessness starts to crack. The passive boom has worked partly because the index has been reshuffled gradually, with new entrants dribbling in at sizes that did not overwhelm the plumbing. When Tesla entered the S&P 500 in December 2020, with a market cap around $600 billion, it was the largest addition ever, and the preparatory trading became a spectacle of front-running that caused visible price distortions. A SpaceX entry, at a valuation potentially double that, would be an order-of-magnitude bigger. The supply of shares available to public investors on day one, however, would be minuscule compared with the trillions of dollars in passive funds that must own the name. In a single week, forced buying could easily push SpaceX’s stock to irrational levels, inflating its index weight, which then triggers additional flows, setting off a mechanical chain reaction with no fundamental anchor.

For index investors, the consequences are insidious. First, the cost of admission to the newly expanded index is paid by every existing holder: when the index adds a sky-high-valuation stock and trims everything else to make room, the fund effectively sells a bit of your Nvidia, your Apple, your Microsoft at market prices and hands you a concentrated chunk of a newly public rocket-and-satellite company at a price that has been run up in anticipation of exactly that trade. The average 401(k) holder will not see the damage until later, when the temporary passive bid fades and the stock finds a real price, potentially far lower.

Second, and more structurally disruptive, SpaceX threatens to break the diversification promise that index funds sell. The S&P 500 is already dangerously top-heavy with technology giants. Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Alphabet and Meta together command more than 30% of the index. If SpaceX joins at a Trillion-plus valuation and becomes another seven or eight percent weight, the passive investor who thinks they own "the market" ends up holding a handful of companies — including a highly volatile space and communications conglomerate with unpredictable regulatory exposures and a chief executive known for erratic public communication. That is not the calm wealth-builder of the brochure.

The Nvidia parallel makes the risk vivid. Nvidia’s ascent has been a triumph for indexers, but only because its profits exploded alongside its valuation. If SpaceX’s earnings do not grow into its IPO price fast enough, the forced absorption of the stock by passive funds will reallocate tens of billions of dollars from proven cash-generating engines like Nvidia into a still-speculative venture, degrading overall returns for years. Paradoxically, the very success of Nvidia in driving index returns now makes the index far more susceptible to a single-company shock, and SpaceX’s arrival would shove another giant lever into a system already precariously balanced.

Skeptics will point out that SpaceX may not qualify for S&P 500 inclusion immediately because of profitability requirements, delaying the day of reckoning. But that only shifts the problem: once profitability is demonstrated — and Starlink appears to be on that trajectory — the market will price in imminent inclusion, and the front-running will begin months in advance. By the time the actual addition occurs, index funds will be buying at the peak of that hype cycle, locking in losses for passive participants unable to opt out.

None of this is to say that index fund investing stops working. It remains a remarkable innovation. But the "set it and forget it" assumption that the 33-year run has never required a second thought may finally need revision. The SpaceX IPO is not just another big listing. It is a stress test of whether passive investing can absorb a company born from the private markets at a scale never before attempted, in an era when indexes have become the tail that wags the entire stock market. For the millions of investors who have simply trusted the arithmetic, the moment that trust gets expensive is drawing closer. And it is Nvidia, the very symbol of passive success, that will be most tellingly affected — its weight reshuffled, its returns diluted, and its role as the quiet engine of index wealth suddenly shared with a rocket ship that might not yet know how to land.
