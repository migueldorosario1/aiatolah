# Inflation Just Spiked to the Highest Level in Almost 3 Years in the First Report Under New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh. But There Was Some Good News



The Inflation Spike and Nvidia: Why the AI Boom Remains Undeterred

The January consumer price index landed like a thunderclap, jolting markets with a headline print that pushed inflation to its highest level in almost three years. The timing could not have been more symbolic: this was the first inflation report issued under the watch of new Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh, a figure known for his deep skepticism of prolonged monetary accommodation. Predictably, the knee-jerk reaction across global markets was brutal, with growth-sensitive stocks absorbing the heaviest blows. Yet, buried within the data and the subsequent policy calculus lies a crucial silver lining—one that reinforces the investment case for Nvidia and the broader artificial intelligence infrastructure buildout.

Headline anxiety centered on a 0.5 percent monthly surge and an annual rate flirting with the upper threes, propelled by stubborn shelter costs and a jump in energy prices. Warsh, who has long argued that the central bank must guard against inflation becoming entrenched, now faces an immediate test of his credibility. Bond yields spiked, and futures rapidly repriced the probability of a rate cut far into 2026. In previous eras, this would have spelled disaster for high-multiple technology names such as Nvidia, whose valuations depend on discounting distant, explosive earnings growth. The script, however, is different this time.

The good news emerged from the composition of the report. Core services excluding housing—a metric the Fed watches obsessively—actually decelerated, signaling that underlying price pressures in the real engine of the economy are not running wild. Wage growth in the technology sector continued to moderate, and rents, which notoriously lag, are expected to roll over in the next few quarters based on already-signed leases. More importantly, market-based inflation expectations, as tracked by five-year breakevens, barely budged after a brief spike. This suggests that while Warsh may lean hawkish in his rhetoric to defend his anti-inflation credentials, the data does not yet demand a radical tightening cycle. A period of holding rates steady—perhaps for all of 2025—is a far cry from the rate-cutting euphoria priced in last year, but it is not a death knell for the AI capital expenditure super-cycle.

That super-cycle is precisely why Nvidia inhabits a universe apart from the typical economic sensitivity that haunts consumer-discretionary or industrial stocks. The world’s hyperscale cloud providers are locked in a strategic arms race to build out the computational backbone for next-generation AI. Their spending decisions are not governed by the monthly ebb and flow of the overnight lending rate. They are funded by long-term debt, immense cash reserves, and the existential imperative to not fall behind in the contest to dominate generative AI. Nvidia’s H200 and Blackwell platforms are booked through multiple quarters, with lead times that stretch deep into next year. The company’s revenue is effectively pre-sold, and its pricing power expands when supply remains tight relative to insatiable demand—a dynamic that higher interest rates do not directly break.

In fact, a stickier inflation environment could paradoxically benefit Nvidia’s margins. If input costs for energy and logistics remain elevated, that validates the premium pricing for its ultra-high-performance chips, as customers have no alternative but to accept the cost if they want to train frontier models. Meanwhile, any slowdown in consumer spending that might result from elevated rates barely touches Nvidia’s enterprise and government client base; the U.S. Department of Energy and sovereign wealth funds funding AI data centers are not canceling orders because mortgage rates are high. The good news from the inflation report, therefore, is that it did not contain a demand-side wage spiral that would threaten the profitability of the exact corporations driving Nvidia’s growth. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon reported capital expenditure guidance in their latest earnings that exceeded even the most optimistic analyst forecasts, and none have signaled any intent to pare back in response to a few extra basis points on the fed funds rate.

For investors, the interplay between Warsh’s new leadership and the stubborn CPI data creates a familiar tale of two markets. The broad S&P 500 index might chop sideways as it digests a higher-for-longer reality. But Nvidia, sitting at the nexus of a technological transformation that transcends monetary policy cycles, represents a pocket of secular resilience. The stock may suffer temporary drawdowns during rates-driven selloffs, yet those episodes are increasingly viewed as entry points by institutional money that recognizes the semiconductor giant’s earnings trajectory is divorced from the traditional economic cycle. With a forward price-to-earnings ratio that has contracted significantly from its 2023 highs even as earnings revisions continue to accelerate upward, the risk-reward skew has rarely been more favorable.

The first inflation report of the Warsh era was undoubtedly a shock. But for those capable of reading beyond the headline, it also confirmed that the AI boom is not a play on imminent rate cuts; it is a structural shift in global capital allocation that will not be derailed by a central bank that is, at worst, merely pausing its easing. Nvidia remains the indispensable supplier in that shift, and that is the most important piece of good news to emerge from an otherwise sobering data release.
