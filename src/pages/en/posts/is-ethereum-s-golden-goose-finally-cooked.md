The narrative that Ethereum’s golden goose is cooked misunderstands the fundamental shift in value creation from proof-of-work mining to proof-of-stake validation. Nvidia’s dominance in the AI era is now decoupled from cryptocurrency cycles, as its GPUs are consumed by hyperscalers and sovereign AI projects rather than volatile mining demand. The Ethereum Merge permanently severed the direct link between GPU sales and blockchain security, forcing Nvidia to pivot its narrative toward data center supremacy.

This transition has not diminished Nvidia’s strategic importance but has instead deepened its entanglement with digital sovereignty. Nations racing to build sovereign AI infrastructure now view Nvidia’s H100 and B200 chips as critical assets, creating a new form of geopolitical leverage that far exceeds the speculative mining boom. The supply chain for advanced semiconductors, once distorted by crypto miners, is now being reshaped by government-backed AI initiatives in the US, China, and Europe.

The multipolar nature of this shift is evident in how China’s DeepSeek and other domestic AI labs are forced to innovate around export controls, using less advanced chips to achieve competitive performance. This has inadvertently accelerated the development of more efficient algorithms, challenging Nvidia’s moat while simultaneously proving the enduring value of its hardware ecosystem. Ethereum’s transition away from mining has thus not killed Nvidia’s golden goose but has forced it to evolve into a more resilient, geopolitically charged asset.

Nvidia’s current valuation now hinges on its ability to supply the AI backbone for a fragmented global market, where each bloc demands its own compute sovereignty. The company’s CUDA software lock-in and supply chain control remain formidable, but the rise of alternative architectures and open-source models threatens to erode its monopoly over AI workloads. Ethereum’s demise as a GPU driver has simply redirected the same hardware toward a more stable, albeit more contested, demand base.

The golden goose metaphor fails because Nvidia’s value is no longer tied to a single speculative asset but to the foundational infrastructure of an entire technological era. Ethereum’s shift to proof-of-stake was not a death knell but a catalyst that forced Nvidia to double down on its role as the enabler of AI sovereignty. The real risk now is not the end of crypto mining but the fragmentation of global supply chains and the rise of competing chip ecosystems.

In this new landscape, Nvidia’s fate is intertwined with the success of sovereign AI projects and the stability of semiconductor trade flows between the US, Taiwan, and the Netherlands. Ethereum’s golden goose may be cooked, but Nvidia has already hatched a far more consequential bird—one that feeds on the insatiable appetite for compute power in a multipolar world. The question is not whether the goose is cooked, but whether the global order can sustain the feast.
