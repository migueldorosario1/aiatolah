The optical networking corner of the tech market roared to life this week after Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang delivered a seemingly simple but profoundly disruptive message: use optics where you must. In a keynote that pivoted from GPUs to the infrastructure stitching them together, Huang made it clear that the next industrial-scale AI buildout will demand a far more intimate marriage between silicon and light. Traders immediately rewired their capital flows, sending Lumentum (LITE), Coherent (COHR) and Corning (GLW) surging in a session where traditional semiconductor names barely moved.

Huang’s directive was not a casual aside. It was a signal, aimed squarely at the hyperscaler and enterprise supply chain, that copper interconnects are approaching a hard performance ceiling for rack-to-rack and data center-scale fabrics. As Nvidia’s GPU clusters balloon beyond 100,000 units, the energy cost and latency of electrical signaling become untenable. Optics, in his framing, cease to be a premium add-on and become a non-negotiable foundation. Lumentum, heavily exposed to high-speed transceivers and laser chips, instantly benefited from the reframing. The company’s recent re-entry into the intra-data-center photonics race suddenly looked prescient rather than reactive.

Coherent, whose vertical integration spans lasers, switches and advanced optical sub-assemblies, gained even more traction. Huang’s implicit endorsement of photonic fabrics essentially validated the thesis that optical switching and micro-transfer printing technology will be as critical as the GPUs themselves. The stock’s jump reflected not just near-term order speculation but a structural re-rating: Coherent is no longer a cyclical supplier, but a long-duration enabler of exponential compute scaling. Corning, often pigeonholed as a specialty glass maker, saw its own surge as investors finally connected its optical fiber and connectivity solutions directly to the AI rack’s nervous system. The company’s ability to produce low-loss, bend-insensitive fiber at scale aligns perfectly with Huang’s “where you must” doctrine—because when the physics demands light, the medium becomes a strategic asset, not a commodity.

The immediate rally in these names is not simply a sympathy trade. It exposes a deeper realization among institutional investors that Nvidia’s roadmap is now an aggressive pull for optical innovation. Every saved watt per terabit, every nanosecond shaved from fabric latency, translates into millions of dollars in avoided infrastructure cost for the end user. Therefore, the pricing power of optical component makers is set to rise asymmetrically, even as GPU ASPs come under eventual pressure from custom ASICs.

However, the sustainability of this move hinges on execution. Coherent and Lumentum must prove they can ship 1.6T and eventually 3.2T transceivers in volume with reliability and power efficiency that no copper link can match. Corning must continue to drive down insertion losses in increasingly dense fiber networks. The Nvidia CEO’s words were a giant air cover, but the real war will be won on yields and integration. If these firms deliver, the surge may be remembered not as a knee-jerk spike, but as the moment optics stopped being an option and became the AI superhighway’s asphalt.
