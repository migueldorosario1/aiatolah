# Marvell Keeps Surging After Nvidia CEO Calls It the Next Trillion-Dollar Company

When Jensen Huang speaks, the market has learned to listen. This week, the Nvidia CEO lent his seal of approval to a company that has quietly become indispensable to the AI revolution, describing Marvell Technology as the next potential trillion-dollar enterprise. The endorsement, delivered during a marquee industry event, sent Marvell shares soaring to fresh highs and reignited the debate about who truly captures value in the age of artificial intelligence.

Huang’s remark was not tossed off lightly. He pointed to Marvell’s deep integration into the custom silicon ecosystem that powers hyperscale data centers. While Nvidia’s GPUs remain the public face of AI training, the inference layer and optimized infrastructure increasingly rely on tailored ASICs co-developed with cloud titans like Amazon, Google, and Microsoft. Marvell sits at the heart of that shift, designing custom compute, networking, and storage solutions that give hyperscalers a path to reduce their dependence on off-the-shelf GPUs while slashing total cost of ownership.

The market reaction underscores a sobering truth: AI infrastructure spending is entering a new phase. As enterprises move from experimentation to deployment, the economics of inference demand efficiency that general-purpose processors alone cannot provide. Marvell’s advanced SerDes, DSP-based optical interconnects, and custom Arm-based compute platforms address exactly that bottleneck. The company’s data-center revenue is on a steep upward trajectory, driven largely by its custom ASIC engagements, which are expected to surpass $800 million in annual revenue this year and accelerate further in 2026.

Huang’s trillion-dollar framing is not mere flattery. With a current market capitalization hovering near $70 billion, Marvell would need a more than fourteenfold appreciation to join the ranks of Nvidia and Apple. Yet the trajectory of companies that sit at the intersection of AI and essential infrastructure suggests the ambition is not fanciful. Broadcom, which maintains a comparable custom-chip franchise, has already surpassed the trillion-dollar mark. Marvell, under CEO Matt Murphy, has methodically repositioned itself from a diversified semiconductor supplier into a pure-play data-infrastructure powerhouse, shedding legacy businesses and concentrating the portfolio around AI, cloud, and 5G.

What makes Marvell’s story especially compelling is its symbiotic relationship with Nvidia rather than a rivalrous one. While Nvidia pushes the frontier of general-purpose AI accelerators, Marvell enables the disaggregated, high-bandwidth fabric that connects them—and increasingly builds the custom logic that runs alongside or even replaces GPUs in specialized workloads. Huang’s public endorsement signals that the AI ecosystem is large enough for multiple winners, and that the real prize lies in enabling the entire data-center stack.

Investors should note that the road to a trillion-dollar valuation is fraught with execution risk. Scaling custom ASIC programs requires deep partnerships, immense engineering resources, and the ability to navigate shifting silicon roadmaps. Marvell has proven it can win and retain hyperscale customers, but sustaining that edge as rivals like Broadcom, AMD, and emerging players ramp their custom efforts will test its operational muscle.

Still, the direction of travel is clear. As AI moves from the laboratory into every layer of enterprise computing, the demand for high-speed, power-efficient infrastructure will only intensify. Marvell, with its unique portfolio and deepening ties to the architects of the cloud, has positioned itself as a prime beneficiary. Huang’s call may prove to be one of the defining investment signals of the current cycle—not because he said it, but because the underlying math of AI infrastructure makes it increasingly plausible.
