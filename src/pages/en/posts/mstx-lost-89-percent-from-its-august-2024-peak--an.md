MSTX Lost 89 Percent From Its August 2024 Peak, and Friday’s Payroll Print Showed Why Leverage Decay Never Stops

When Defiance launched the Daily Target 2X Long Nvidia ETF under the ticker MSTX in August 2024, it promised a straightforward proposition: double the daily return of Nvidia stock. For the first few days, that promise worked spectacularly as Nvidia shares surged past $130 on AI euphoria. The fund peaked on August 23, rewarding early speculators with a quick gain. Then the mathematics of leverage took over. As of this writing, MSTX has collapsed 89 percent from that August high, a ride that has destroyed far more capital than even a simple 2x decline in the underlying would imply. The culprit is not just the pullback in Nvidia, but a brutal sequence of volatile trading sessions, the most recent of which arrived with Friday’s payroll print and once again illustrated why leveraged ETFs are designed to decay, never to recover lost ground symmetrically.

The mechanics are well-documented but easily ignored in bull markets. A fund like MSTX uses swaps and futures to target double the daily return of NVDA before fees. Because it resets every night, a down day followed by an equal percentage up day does not return the portfolio to its starting point. A 5 percent drop in Nvidia erases roughly 10 percent from MSTX. The next day, even if Nvidia rebounds 5.26 percent to fully recover its loss, MSTX gains only 10.52 percent from a lower base, leaving it about 0.5 percent short of breakeven. Over weeks and months, this gap widens into a chasm, especially when the underlying stock is prone to large swings in both directions. Nvidia, for all its long-term success, has become one of the most volatile mega-cap names on earth, with routine intraday moves of 3 to 8 percent driven by geopolitical headlines, export restrictions, earnings surprises, and macroeconomic data.

Friday’s March nonfarm payrolls report gave the market exactly the kind of shock that accelerates decay. The Labor Department reported that the economy added 228,000 jobs, beating consensus estimates and, more importantly, signaling that wage growth remains firm. Treasury yields jumped instantly, with the 10-year breaching levels not seen since the early months of the tightening cycle. In a yield-sensitive equity landscape, the immediate translation was a rotation out of high-duration growth names. Nvidia shed over 7 percent intraday, dragging MSTX down more than 14 percent within hours. For a fund already nursing deep wounds, that single session inflicted damage that a calm recovery day would never fully reverse.

The 89 percent loss is not an anomaly confined to MSTX; it is the unavoidable consequence of holding a product built on daily leverage through a period of elevated realized volatility. Nvidia itself has fallen roughly 35 percent from its summer 2024 top, but MSTX’s drawdown is more than two and a half times larger. The explanation lies in the path dependency: Nvidia’s decline has been anything but a straight line. The stock has bounced violently multiple times — on DeepSeek panic, on trade-war threats, on earnings beats — each bounce temporarily lifting MSTX from a diminished level before the next sell-off carved out a new bottom. Every round trip between hope and fear exacted a toll that cannot be recouped unless the underlying embarks on a sustained, low-volatility rally. That has not happened.

Friday’s payroll print delivered a clear reminder that macroeconomic catalysts still dictate the rhythm of the AI trade. Strong labor data keeps the Federal Reserve on hold, pushes rate-cut expectations further into the future, and strengthens the dollar, all of which act as headwinds for richly valued semiconductor names. For a leveraged fund, the problem is not merely directional; it is the recurring turbulence that chops up the compounding math. Each time a jobs report, an inflation reading, or a tariff escalation sends Nvidia into a tailspin, MSTX pays the price twice over, and the subsequent bounce — however vigorous — repairs only a fraction of the damage.

The pain is also a lesson about time horizon. The prospectus of any leveraged ETF explicitly warns that the product is intended for single-day holding periods, yet MSTX’s average volume suggests a large number of retail traders have treated it as a longer-term bet on Nvidia’s AI dominance. That conviction may prove correct over a multi-year arc, but the vehicle itself is structurally incapable of delivering that return without catastrophic erosion along the way. An investor who bought at the August top believing in Nvidia’s ultimate future would now need an advance of more than 800 percent in MSTX just to break even. Even if Nvidia recovers and surpasses its old highs with a steady climb, MSTX will most likely trail behind because the volatility that accumulates on the journey upward is just as punishing as that on the way down.

Friday’s reaction was a microcosm of this dynamic. The surprisingly strong payroll number did not fundamentally alter Nvidia’s intrinsic value, but it triggered a sharp repricing of risk that clobbered the leveraged fund. Next week, if the CPI print comes in soft or if the White House signals a de-escalation on chip sanctions, Nvidia could easily rally 10 percent, and MSTX would pop 20 percent. Yet even that improbable snapback would leave the fund catastrophically below its launch-era peak, and the churn between data points means the decay never stops, it simply gets a new coat of paint before the next drawdown.

For anyone still holding MSTX, the question is no longer about Nvidia’s earnings power or the long-term outlook for artificial intelligence. It is about the relentless arithmetic of daily rebalancing in a world where payrolls, tariffs, and geopolitical whispers can send a stock flying in both directions on consecutive days. The fund’s near-total collapse from its August peak is not a verdict on Nvidia’s prospects; it is proof that leverage decay is a force that operates continuously, ruthlessly, and without pause, accelerated by precisely the kind of macro-driven volatility that Friday’s numbers so vividly delivered.
