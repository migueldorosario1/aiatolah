# NBIS’s 16% Plunge: A Buying Opportunity or a Warning Sign? Nvidia’s Robotics Embrace Hints at the Former

Nebius Group (NBIS) has shed more than 16% in a week, a stumble that tests the conviction of early investors in the AI infrastructure pure-play. The sell-off, partly tied to broader tech rotation and post-restructuring volatility, masks a quieter but potentially transformative development: the company’s Nvidia-backed robotics unit is beginning to turn industry heads. For those tracking Nvidia’s expanding ecosystem, this is not a sideshow—it’s the main event.

The robotics bet sits inside Nebius’s fast-growing AI cloud and infrastructure arm, leveraging a privileged relationship with Nvidia. While many AI infrastructure names are battling for GPU allocations, Nebius has secured direct access to Nvidia’s latest H200 and, soon, Blackwell chips, funneled not only into its data centers but directly into a dedicated robotics lab. There, the team is building upon Nvidia’s Isaac platform and Omniverse digital twins to develop autonomous mobile robots and manipulation systems aimed at logistics and industrial automation.

Why does Nvidia’s stamp matter so much here? Because Jensen Huang has made it clear: the next trillion-dollar wave is “physical AI” — embodied intelligence that navigates, perceives, and acts in the real world. Nvidia doesn’t sprinkle its name on every robotics project; it strategically partners where it sees a path to vertical integration and scale. Nebius, with its engineering talent pool inherited from Yandex and a freshly cleaned corporate structure, offers a fast track to deploying such systems in the vast warehousing and manufacturing sectors of EMEA and beyond. The optics of a post-Blackwell Nvidia backing this venture have already sparked inquiry from institutional allocators tired of pure-play cloud compute stories.

So why is the stock still falling? The answer lies more in market mechanics than fundamentals. NBIS enjoyed a fierce rally after escaping its Russian entanglement, and the current pullback looks like a classic mix of profit-taking and caution ahead of the company’s first full earnings after the reboot. Add a wave of skepticism toward unprofitable AI infrastructure names, and the short-term pressure is understandable. Yet, the narrative is shifting under investors’ feet. The robotics angle is not priced into a stock that most traders still view as a simple GPU-for-hire play.

If Nvidia’s robotics thesis is even half right, the addressable market for autonomous embodied systems will dwarf today’s cloud AI spend. Nebius, with its tightly integrated hardware-software approach and Nvidia’s co-development muscle, is positioning itself as a key conduit. This is not a guaranteed win — robotics is a graveyard of overhyped pilots — but the presence of Nvidia’s full stack, from training chips to simulation, dramatically shortens the odds.

For investors willing to look past a week of red ink, the NBIS sell-off might be the reset needed to enter a differentiated AI story. The Nvidia-backed robotics bet isn’t just a curiosity; it could be the catalyst that turns a beaten-down stock into the next chapter of the AI revolution. Ignore it at your own peril.
