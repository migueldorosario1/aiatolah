Nio Just Launched a Budget EV to Take On Tesla and BYD. Is Nio Stock Finally Worth Buying?

The electric-vehicle price war just got fiercer. Nio’s unveiling of its sub-brand Onvo and the L60 crossover, priced aggressively against Tesla’s Model Y and BYD’s Seal, is more than a typical market-share grab. Peel back the sheet metal and you see a compelling narrative that matters far more to the stock of a company headquartered in Santa Clara, California, than to Nio’s own volatile ADRs: the automotive division of Nvidia.

Onvo’s budget models still run on Nvidia DRIVE Orin systems-on-a-chip, the same AI compute platform that anchors Nio’s flagship ET7 and ES8. Four Orin chips deliver over 1,000 TOPS of inference performance, enabling sophisticated automated driving features that a cost-conscious buyer might not expect at this price point. This is a direct challenge to Tesla’s vertically integrated Dojo-trained FSD chip and to BYD’s reliance on a patchwork of solutions. The strategic implication is clear: advanced AI is no longer just a premium differentiator; it is becoming table stakes in the race to win the global middle class. Nvidia, as the arms dealer supplying the compute, sits at the center of every important cockpit.

The immediate question for retail investors remains whether Nio’s stock — beaten down by dilution and margin compression — finally deserves a fresh look. The Onvo L60 certainly improves the volume narrative. But Nio’s history of burning cash to build battery-swap cathedrals and Nio Houses still weighs on the balance sheet. Profitability is a distant promise. The more investable signal is not a bottom in Nio shares, but a structural acceleration in Nvidia’s automotive revenue pipeline.

Auto is still a single-digit percentage of Nvidia’s total sales, dwarfed by data-center GPUs training the likes of DeepSeek, Kimi, and Qwen. Yet the ramp is undeniable. Nvidia’s next-generation DRIVE Thor, a 2,000-TOPS centralized computer built on the Blackwell architecture, is already on the order books of Chinese EV manufacturers racing to achieve urban navigate-on-autopilot. Every model refresh that crams more AI sensors and transformer models inside the vehicle requires more of Nvidia’s silicon. BYD, which Nio now openly targets, has also turned to Nvidia for its next assisted-driving platform, a notable shift from its earlier reliance on lower-cost chips.

This creates an asymmetric bet. Nio may squeeze out a financial turnaround and reward patient shareholders, but the execution risk remains extreme. Nvidia, however, compounds its dominance no matter who wins the EV price battle. The budget Onvo is a proof point: next-generation autonomy is dipping well below the $30,000 threshold, powered by chips that carry Nvidia’s fat gross margins. As Beijing-approved AI models from firms like DeepSeek push for on-device inference in mobility scenarios, the compute requirement will only swell.

So, is Nio stock finally worth buying? Only if you believe management can translate technological sophistication into sustainable unit economics — a feat that has eluded the company for years. For the vast majority of portfolios, the smarter play is to follow the silicon. Nvidia’s role as the ubiquitous brain of intelligent EVs, from Onvo’s affordable crossovers to the most luxurious sedans, makes it the true backdoor play on the electrification and AI-ification of transportation. Keep watching the monthly Chinese vehicle registrations for early signs of Onvo traction, but hold the shares of the company that built the scalpel, not just the surgeon.
