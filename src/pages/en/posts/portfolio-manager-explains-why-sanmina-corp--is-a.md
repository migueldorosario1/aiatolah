Portfolio Manager Explains Why Sanmina Corp. Is a Good Buy

In the rapidly expanding universe of artificial intelligence, Nvidia commands the spotlight as the essential provider of GPU computing power. Yet seasoned investors understand that the real value often lies one step removed, in the specialized manufacturing partners that transform chip designs into physical reality. Sanmina Corporation, a global electronics manufacturing services company, is precisely that kind of opportunity, and one portfolio manager is making a compelling case for why the stock deserves a place in AI-focused portfolios.

The investment thesis rests on Sanmina’s deep and expanding relationship with Nvidia. As demand for H100 and upcoming Blackwell GPUs surges past all forecasts, Nvidia’s reliance on capable manufacturing partners intensifies. Sanmina provides high-complexity printed circuit board assembly, backplane interconnects, and integrated systems that are critical to the GPU clusters powering data centers worldwide. This is not commodity manufacturing; it involves precision engineering for high-speed, high-density computing environments where signal integrity and thermal management are paramount.

What makes Sanmina particularly attractive, according to the portfolio manager, is the asymmetric risk-reward profile. The company’s valuation remains grounded at a fraction of Nvidia’s multiples, yet its revenue stream is becoming increasingly tied to the same AI megatrend. Sanmina’s healthcare, automotive, and communications segments provide diversification, but the AI-driven data center business is the growth engine. With Nvidia’s supply chain capacity still constrained, Sanmina’s role as a proven, scaled partner becomes more indispensable. Every additional GPU that Nvidia ships requires supporting infrastructure, much of which Sanmina builds.

The portfolio manager also highlights Sanmina’s improving capital efficiency. The company has been systematically divesting lower-margin segments and reinvesting in advanced manufacturing capabilities, particularly in its defense and aerospace unit, which benefits from heightened geopolitical tensions. This dual exposure to commercial AI infrastructure and government-funded modernization programs creates a moat that few competitors can replicate. The balance sheet supports this pivot: net debt is manageable, and free cash flow generation remains robust, allowing both organic investment and shareholder returns through buybacks.

For investors seeking to capture the AI hardware boom without chasing Nvidia at elevated valuations, Sanmina offers a pragmatic route. The correlation is direct but the price tag is far more grounded. As Nvidia’s production ramps up in the wake of the Blackwell platform launch, the portfolio manager expects Sanmina’s guidance to move higher, potentially re-rating the stock as the market better appreciates its position within the AI supply chain. The trade is simple: you can buy the shovel maker at 50 times earnings, or you can buy the engineer who designs and assembles the shovel at a single-digit multiple. In a bull market for artificial intelligence, both should work, but the latter starts with far more room to run.
