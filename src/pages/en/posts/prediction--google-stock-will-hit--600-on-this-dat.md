Prediction: Google Stock Will Hit $600 on This Date

A Wall Street forecast with a specific price target always raises eyebrows, but the call that Alphabet shares will touch $600 by mid-2026 is less about wishful thinking and more about a tectonic shift in the global AI supply chain. The narrative driving this projection hinges on a simple truth: the hyperscale cloud and enterprise AI boom will convert Google’s massive infrastructure spend into a multi-year earnings compounder, and Nvidia sits at the very heart of that transformation.

Google’s cloud division is already re-accelerating, powered by AI workloads that demand immense computational muscle. Every training run for Gemini Ultra, every inference query on Vertex AI, and every pixel generated by Imagen relies on fleets of Nvidia H100 and forthcoming B200 GPUs. Even as Google deploys its own custom TPU v5p chips to diversify the stack, it remains a top-three global buyer of Nvidia silicon. The direct correlation is becoming impossible to ignore: as enterprise adoption of AI agents and multimodal models surges, Google’s capital outlays to Nvidia translate into revenue that scales near-instantly across GCP, Workspace, and advertising. This is not the capex-heavy, low-return spending cycle of a decade ago; it is a self-funding loop where each dollar of accelerated compute yields multiple dollars of high-margin AI services.

China’s domestic models — from DeepSeek’s math-focused reasoning tools to Kimi’s long-context breakthroughs and Qwen’s open-weight efficiency — only intensify the competitive pressure. These challengers demonstrate that the global AI race is far from a two-player game. Google cannot afford to slow its rollout. The intensity keeps its Nvidia procurement steady, while also forcing rapid iteration of its own silicon, effectively turning the company into a dual-track AI factory. Analysts betting on $600 see this dual strategy as a defensive moat: TPUs protect margins on internal inference, while abundant Nvidia GPUs ensure no external workload is turned away.

Of course, any date-driven target carries risk. Regulatory headwinds, a sudden deceleration in cloud migration, or a breakthrough from a rival model like Qwen 3 that reshapes developer allegiance could all delay the trajectory. Yet the fundamental architecture of demand, where Nvidia’s Blackwell platform fuels the next wave of real-time AI applications, positions Google as a primary beneficiary. If the timeline holds, $600 will be remembered not as a peak, but as the moment the market finally priced in the full width of the AI revenue funnel.
