The prospect of a single company ballooning to nearly one-fifth of a widely held index fund sounds like a thought experiment from the peak of a speculative mania. Yet that is precisely the scenario now being whispered across trading desks: before the end of 2026, SpaceX could represent 19% of a low-cost Vanguard ETF, radically reshaping its risk profile and testing the limits of passive investing. This is not a fantasy about a far-flung space exploration fund. It is a direct, mathematical consequence of how market-cap-weighted indices work, and it arrives on the heels of a similar phenomenon already playing out with Nvidia.

Nvidia provides the playbook. When a single stock races to a multi-trillion-dollar valuation on the back of an epochal technological shift, it does not merely join an index; it conquers it. The Vanguard S&P 500 ETF (VOO) now holds Nvidia at a weight surpassing 6%, and the Vanguard Information Technology ETF (VGT) recently had to rebalance because Nvidia breached the 25% mark. This was not a malfunction of the rules, but a feature. If artificial intelligence can push a chip designer to such extremes, the argument goes, then the commercial space economy can do the same for its undisputed champion once SpaceX enters public markets.

The mechanism is simple. Should Elon Musk’s company pursue a direct listing or initial public offering at a valuation north of $200 billion — which is conservative given current secondary market activity and its Starlink cash flows — it would instantly qualify for major benchmarks. A $1 trillion valuation by late 2026, driven by Starship milestones and a mature satellite internet monopoly, is well within the realm of possibility. At that size, a broad market or large-cap growth Vanguard fund with a limited number of names could see SpaceX gobble up weight from Apple, Microsoft, and yes, Nvidia. The 19% figure assumes a fund that either concentrates in a few truly dominant growth names or deliberately tilts toward the next industrial revolution. The Vanguard Mega Cap Growth ETF (MGK), for instance, holds just over 70 stocks. A single trillion-dollar debutant would immediately become its crown jewel.

The Nvidia parallel demands attention because the semiconductor giant has already normalized extreme concentration. Investors who cheered Nvidia’s ascent to becoming the S&P 500’s largest driver of returns are now grappling with the hidden cost: amplification of idiosyncratic risk. If a government export control or an earnings miss can shave hundreds of billions from Nvidia’s value, what happens when a fledgling stock exposed to launch failures, geopolitical tugs-of-war over spectrum rights, and a mercurial founder commands 19% of your retirement fund? The Vanguard ETF in question would cease to offer broad diversification. It would become a leveraged bet on a single corporate entity whose fortunes are tied to literal rocket science.

There is a second layer of discomfort for passive purists. SpaceX’s inclusion would not be a slow creep but a violent event. On the day of index addition, the ETF would need to acquire billions in shares, likely at a peak price inflated by front-running and forced buying. The very cheapness that defines Vanguard’s low-cost appeal belies a structural moment where the tail wags the dog. Liquidity, valuation discipline, and the benign ignorance of the indexer all get thrown into the cargo bay and jettisoned.

This prediction is not a warning against SpaceX. On the contrary, it is a tribute to the sheer economic force the company could become. It is, however, a red flag about the machinery of passive investing that Nvidia has already stress-tested. When a single theme captures the market’s imagination, the plumbing of our portfolios cannot stay indifferent. By 2026, the ghost of Nvidia’s dominance will likely be joined by a fleet of Starlink satellites reflecting a hard truth back to Earth: diversification in the age of moonshots is a fragile promise.
