SMH ETF Investors: Watch Hyperscaler Capex Guidance at July Earnings Calls

The $25 billion VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) is up more than 50% over the past twelve months, powered by the artificial intelligence boom and a nearly 10% weighting in Nvidia. For investors holding SMH, the single most critical set of events in July is not a product launch or a Federal Reserve meeting. It is the second-quarter earnings calls from the four U.S. hyperscale cloud providers — Microsoft, Amazon, Alphabet and Meta Platforms. Their capital expenditure guidance will determine whether Nvidia and the broader semiconductor complex have enough demand momentum to justify current valuations, or whether the AI infrastructure trade is finally running out of steam.

Hyperscaler capex is the direct pipeline to Nvidia’s data center revenue. In the most recent quarter, Nvidia reported that sales to large cloud providers represented roughly 40% of its data center business. When Amazon Web Services decides to accelerate its procurement of H100 and next-generation Blackwell chips, that order flows through Dell, Supermicro and ultimately Nvidia’s top line. When Alphabet’s Google Cloud signals that it will keep spending at a $12 billion quarterly run-rate in 2025, it sends a wave of confidence across the entire GPU supply chain. Conversely, any hint of moderation or a shift toward an “optimization year” immediately raises the specter of overcapacity and a painful inventory correction.

That is precisely what makes the July reporting window so pivotal. Microsoft will report on July 30, followed by Meta on July 31, with Amazon and Alphabet due in early August. Last quarter, all four companies surprised to the upside on capex, collectively spending more than $47 billion, up 42% year-over-year. Their 2024 full-year outlooks were raised, and executives used earnings calls to stress that the risk of underinvesting in AI is far greater than the risk of overinvesting. SMH promptly rallied another 15% in the following three weeks. The question now is whether that narrative holds.

There are reasons for caution. Returns on AI investment remain largely intangible for some applications, and Wall Street is beginning to scrutinize the timeline for monetization. Microsoft’s Copilot and Meta’s AI assistants have yet to deliver a clear revenue inflection, while Google is still integrating Gemini across a massive advertising ecosystem. If any of the Big Tech CFOs dial back capex guidance by even a few percentage points — perhaps signaling a pause to absorb capacity or a pivot to internal chip designs — the reaction in Nvidia’s stock, and therefore SMH, will be amplified by high options positioning and elevated expectations.

For SMH investors, the playbook is not to guess the direction but to recognize that these earnings calls are the fundamental catalyst that will override seasonality, technical patterns and even product news. Nvidia’s Blackwell ramp may be on track, but its volume trajectory depends entirely on the purchase orders that hyperscale treasurers approve. A single cautious phrase from an AWS executive — “we see an opportunity to optimize our fleet before the next expansion” — would be enough to trigger a pullback in SMH, given the ETF’s concentrated exposure. All five of SMH’s top holdings — Nvidia, TSMC, Broadcom, ASML and AMD — derive a significant portion of their valuation premium from the assumption that hyperscale capex will grow by at least 25% in 2025.

That assumption will be tested in late July. Investors should listen not just for the headline capex number but for commentary on the mix. A shift toward custom silicon (Google’s TPUs, Amazon’s Trainium, Microsoft’s Maia) could be incrementally negative for Nvidia and ASML, even if total spending rises. Similarly, comments about the pace of liquid-cooled data center deployments will influence Supermicro and other SMH components. The key is to triangulate the guidance across all four calls. If three of the four raise spending targets and sound unequivocally bullish, SMH will likely resume its rally. If two sound cautious, the ETF may enter a prolonged consolidation.

The July earnings calls are not just another checkpoint. They are the moment when the market will reconcile the near-term reality of AI spending with the long-duration equity story embedded in semiconductor stocks. SMH investors who treat these calls as background noise will be reacting to price moves after the fact. Those who parse the capex signal in real time will have the advantage in managing risk for what has become the most concentrated momentum trade in the market.
