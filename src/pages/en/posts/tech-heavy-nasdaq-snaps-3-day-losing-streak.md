The Nasdaq Composite arrested a three-session slide on Tuesday, propelled by a sharp rebound in semiconductor shares, with Nvidia anchoring the recovery. The AI bellwether surged more than 6%, adding nearly $150 billion in market capitalization, as institutional buyers stepped in after a 13% drawdown from last month’s record high. The move signals that despite lingering rate anxiety, conviction remains robust around the generative AI infrastructure buildout and Nvidia’s central role in it.

Pressure had mounted earlier in the week as hotter-than-expected economic data fanned fears that the Federal Reserve would keep rates elevated longer, punishing richly valued growth names. The Nasdaq’s three-day rout wiped roughly $1.2 trillion from the tech sector, with Nvidia alone shedding roughly $400 billion as momentum traders unwound positions. Yet Tuesday’s reversal suggests that deep-pocketed investors view the pullback as a tactical reset rather than a thematic breakdown. Order-flow data showed a flood of call buying in Nvidia out-of-the-money strike prices, wagering on upside into next month’s GTC conference where the company is expected to unveil its next-generation Blackwell architecture.

Nvidia’s gravitational pull on the Nasdaq is now unparalleled. The stock contributes over 8% of the index’s weight, and its correlation with AI-derivative plays—from server OEMs to enterprise software vendors—turns any Nvidia rally into a broad-spectrum tailwind for the composite. Tuesday’s rebound rippled through the supply chain: AMD, Broadcom, and TSMC all closed higher, while memory and networking names staged their own recoveries. The synchronized bounce underscores how deeply the market’s risk appetite is tethered to Nvidia’s growth narrative, which remains firmly intact with hyperscaler capex guidance for 2025 accelerating and sovereign AI demand compounding.

Skeptics point to the nascent push from cheaper Chinese alternatives like DeepSeek and Qwen as a potential margin threat at the inference layer. Yet the numbers remain overwhelmingly in Nvidia’s favor: enterprise transition to hopper-scale workloads is still in the first third of a multi-year cycle, and Blackwell’s performance leap entrenches the CUDA ecosystem’s lock-in. For now, the Nasdaq’s reprieve reads less as a bear-market bounce and more as a conviction re-pricing—a recognition that in an era of exponential compute demand, Nvidia’s scarcity premium endures. The onus now shifts to CEO Jensen Huang’s keynote. If history is any guide, the Nasdaq’s three-day losing streak may look, in hindsight, like little more than a loading phase before the next leg higher.
