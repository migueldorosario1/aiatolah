<strong>The $200 Billion Reason Amazon Could Be an Overlooked AI Winner</strong>

When discussion turns to the artificial intelligence boom, the spotlight rarely strays from Nvidia. The chipmaker’s H100 and forthcoming Blackwell GPUs command the premium training market, and its data center revenue has become the benchmark by which AI exuberance is measured. Yet tucked inside Amazon’s sprawling empire is a quiet force that may ultimately capture a larger slice of the AI value chain than the market appreciates. The reason is a $200 billion commitment that goes far beyond buying Nvidia silicon.

That number reflects the cumulative capital Amazon Web Services is plowing into AI infrastructure over a multiyear horizon. CFO Brian Olsavsky has signaled that capex will step up significantly in 2025, with the “vast majority” directed at AWS to support generative AI workloads. Analysts from Bernstein and Evercore project that Amazon’s total capex could jump to $70 billion or more next year, and sustain that elevated pace as new data center campuses come online. Over the coming decade, the bill for AI-first cloud capacity could comfortably exceed $200 billion. This is not merely rent-seeking on Nvidia’s coat-tails; it is a bet that ownership of the AI stack — from custom silicon to cloud services — will be the decisive moat.

Amazon’s overlooked advantage lies in its dual-track silicon strategy. While AWS remains the largest buyer of Nvidia GPUs in the cloud, it has also designed its own AI training and inference chips. Trainium2, now in production, and the forthcoming Trainium3 aim to offer price-performance ratios that Nvidia’s general-purpose accelerators cannot match for certain workloads. When Anthropic — a frontier model builder and AWS customer — commits to training future models on Trainium clusters, it signals that custom silicon is no longer just a cost-cutting footnote but a credible alternative. By vertically integrating chip design, Amazon reduces its long-term dependency on Nvidia’s supply constraints and pricing power, a structural edge that pure GPU resellers lack.

The financial architecture of this bet is purpose-built. AWS operates on a model where upfront infrastructure spending converts into recurring, high-margin revenue streams. Each new AI data center filled with both Nvidia GPUs and Amazon-designed accelerators becomes a magnet for enterprises that prefer to rent rather than build. Unlike Nvidia, which sells a capital asset once, AWS generates a lifetime of revenue from the software, security, and data management layers that surround AI workloads. By offering the broadest menu of silicon — Intel, AMD, Nvidia, and its own — AWS captures customers with varying cost and performance needs, while the $200 billion buildout ensures it can host the next generation of models without conceding market share to Microsoft Azure or Google Cloud.

The market’s fixation on Nvidia’s earnings as the sole AI barometer undervalues this position. Nvidia’s success is, paradoxically, a catalyst for AWS. Every enterprise that wants to train or fine-tune models on GPUs will need cloud capacity, and AWS remains the largest infrastructure-as-a-service provider. Even if some of that capacity uses Nvidia chips, the orchestration of storage, networking, and database services layers on additional AWS revenue that Nvidia cannot touch. In effect, Amazon partakes in the AI buildout regardless of whose silicon wins the benchmark wars.

What of the risk that hyperscaler capex becomes a commodity sinkhole? Amazon’s retail DNA — obsessive cost control and scale economics — makes it the most disciplined of the cloud trio. Its custom chip program is a testament to that discipline. By shifting internal AI workloads and third-party customers to homegrown silicon, AWS can moderate the margin compression that pure GPU cloud hosting would impose. And unlike a startup that must fund infrastructure with equity, Amazon’s cash-generating retail and advertising businesses provide a backstop during the investment cycle.

There is a further, less obvious payoff. The $200 billion buildout cements AWS as the operating system for enterprise AI. Once a company entrusts its data to AWS and builds on Bedrock, SageMaker, or Amazon’s foundational models, switching costs climb. The same infrastructure that powers high-margin AI inference workloads also entrenches Amazon’s data lake, identity management, and analytics services. It is a classic land-grab strategy, and one that turns the AI capex cycle from a speculative gamble into a structural widening of the competitive moat.

Investors who view AI solely through the lens of semiconductor unit sales are missing this story. Nvidia provides the picks and shovels, but Amazon is building the mine. The $200 billion infrastructure commitment represents a formidable barrier to entry that few competitors can replicate, and it positions AWS to be the indispensable utility of the AI era. While Nvidia will deservedly remain in the limelight, Amazon is quietly reengineering its business to be the overlooked but durable winner of the intelligence revolution.
