The Indicator That Predicted 1929 and 2000 Is Flashing Red — and Nvidia Is at the Epicenter

Long before algorithms and AI-generated trading signals, a quiet metric developed by Nobel laureate Robert Shiller quietly mapped the two most devastating stock market collapses in modern history. The cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings ratio, or CAPE, smooths out corporate profits over a decade to filter out noise and reveal when equities have detached from economic reality. In the summer of 1929, it surged past 30. In the final months before the dot-com bust, it kissed 44. Both episodes ended with the S&P 500 losing roughly half its value. Today, the CAPE ratio is once again above 35, a rarefied altitude that has never offered a soft landing. And this time, the gravitational center of that exuberance is a single semiconductor company: Nvidia.

Nvidia’s ascent from a graphics-card specialist to the indispensable engine of the artificial intelligence revolution has been nothing short of breathtaking. Its market value has swollen beyond three trillion dollars, briefly making it the most valuable corporation on the planet. The narrative fueling this run is compelling: AI will rewrite every industry, and Nvidia’s chips are the picks and shovels of that transformation. The logic is impeccable in a qualitative sense, but markets are not pricing machines for stories — they are discounting mechanisms for future cash flows. And the implied cash flows needed to justify Nvidia’s current multiple belong more to science fiction than to discounted cash flow models.

At its peak in early 2000, Cisco Systems carried a price-to-earnings ratio that defied gravity. The internet was real, and Cisco’s routers were genuinely critical. Yet the stock collapsed 86% from its high, not because the thesis was wrong, but because the valuation had borrowed from a future that arrived too slowly. Nvidia trades at a similar narrative premium. Even after its latest correction, the forward earnings multiple hovers in territory that assumes AI capital spending will grow exponentially for years without interruption, competition will remain dormant, and hyperscalers will never pause their buying spree. History suggests that such perfection is rarely delivered on schedule.

The CAPE ratio’s alarm is not about Nvidia in isolation. High market concentration amplifies the danger. With a handful of mega-cap tech stocks now comprising an unprecedented share of the S&P 500, a deflation of the AI trade would not be a contained sector rotation. It would be a systemic event, dragging down indices, pension funds, and the exchange-traded products that have made passive investing a crowded and correlated trade. In 1929, the culprit was a credit-fueled frenzy in industrial conglomerates and utilities. In 2000, it was the dot-coms. Today, the entire edifice rests on the assumption that a single company can continue to grow faster than whole economies while maintaining margins that invite regulatory and competitive responses.

None of this is a prediction that Nvidia will collapse tomorrow. Bull markets can defy gravity longer than skeptics can remain solvent, and the AI megatrend is genuinely transformative. But the CAPE ratio has been flashing red with a director’s cut of the same movie. It called the peak ahead of the Great Depression. It called the top three months before the Nasdaq began its 78% plunge. It would be intellectually negligent to dismiss its signal simply because the scenery this time looks different. The indicator is not a crystal ball, but it is a mirror reflecting the price of collective enthusiasm — and right now, that reflection shows an image strikingly similar to the past two times euphoria ended in tears. Investors who choose to look away from Nvidia’s valuation prism are not avoiding risk; they are just refusing to read the subtitles.
