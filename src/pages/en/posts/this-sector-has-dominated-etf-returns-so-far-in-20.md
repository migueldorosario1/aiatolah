Amid the market turbulence that has defined the opening half of 2026, one sector has reasserted its dominance over exchange-traded fund returns with an almost gravitational pull: artificial intelligence hardware, epitomized and amplified by Nvidia. As global investors recalibrate for a higher-for-longer rate environment and geopolitical fragmentation, the AI infrastructure build-out has not merely continued—it has accelerated, turning semiconductor ETFs into the undisputed champions of this year’s performance league tables.

At the heart of this rally lies Nvidia, whose weight in bellwether funds such as the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) and the iShares Semiconductor ETF (SOXX) has acted as a supercharger. Nvidia’s market capitalization journey through 2026, driven by the ramp of its Blackwell Ultra platform and the early sampling of the next-generation Vera CPU-Rubin GPU architecture, has once again caught consensus expectations off guard. Hyperscalers and sovereign AI clouds are absorbing every available H200 and B200 accelerator, while the software ecosystem around CUDA, Omniverse, and Nvidia AI Enterprise creates a widening competitive moat that direct rival AMD and in-house ASIC efforts from cloud titans have yet to breach. Consequently, SMH has surged over 40% year-to-date, dwarfing the broader S&P 500’s single-digit advance and even outpacing concentrated AI software baskets.

Yet the dominance is not merely a single-stock story. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, the indispensable silicon foundry for Nvidia’s chips, has seen its own valuation expand as CoWoS advanced packaging capacity finally catches up with demand, easing supply bottlenecks that plagued 2025. ETFs tracking the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index have thus benefited from a powerful double engine: Nvidia’s design leadership and TSMC’s manufacturing execution. Smaller peers like ASML and Broadcom have also contributed, but the performance attribution sharply points to the AI accelerator ecosystem.

The Chinese model makers—DeepSeek, Kimi, and Qwen—add a fascinating layer to the 2026 narrative. The concern earlier this year that low-cost, high-efficiency Chinese large language models might dampen demand for premium GPUs has been turned on its head. Instead of reducing the appetite for Nvidia’s highest-margin products, the proliferation of these models across Asia’s enterprises and mobile ecosystems has broadened the total addressable market. Cloud providers in South Korea, Japan, and the Middle East are now racing to host inference workloads for these families of models, requiring massive Nvidia H200 clusters for real-time responsiveness. Moreover, the continued U.S. export controls have funneled Chinese AI demand into the permitted B200A and tailored chips, sustaining Nvidia’s indirect revenue streams while pushing local developers like Alibaba’s Qwen to innovate within wattage constraints. Far from a threat, the Chinese AI renaissance has validated the indispensability of Nvidia’s compute platform, and semiconductor ETFs have reaped the upside.

Investors are now asking whether this sector dominance can persist into the second half of 2026. The bull case rests on the enterprise AI capex cycle, which JPMorgan estimates still has at least 18 months of positive momentum. Nvidia’s upcoming Vera CPU and NVSwitch 6 fabric promise to solidify its data-center total cost of ownership lead. The bear case—that a digestion phase or a macro-induced slowdown in cloud spending will compress valuations—cannot be dismissed, given that SMH’s forward price-to-earnings ratio now sits near 28, a premium to its five-year average. Yet in a market where duration risk remains penalized and growth is scarce, the AI hardware sector offers a compelling blend of cyclical momentum and secular duration that ETF flows are predictably chasing.

For portfolio managers, the 2026 lesson is unambiguous: semiconductor ETFs, with Nvidia as their lodestar, have stopped being merely cyclical instruments. They have become the primary exchange-traded expression of global technological transformation, absorbing capital that might otherwise seek safety in low-volatility consumer staples. As long as the artificial intelligence supercycle maintains its capex trajectory and Nvidia continues executing across hardware and software, the sector will likely extend its reign over ETF returns, setting a new benchmark for theme-driven investing.
