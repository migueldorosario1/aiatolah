The race to build semiconductor factories in Southeast Asia has intensified, with both the United States and China now targeting Indonesia as a key manufacturing hub. This strategic push reflects a global scramble to diversify chip production beyond traditional centers like Taiwan and South Korea.

American companies are investing heavily in new facilities in Batam and other Indonesian industrial zones, aiming to secure supply chains away from geopolitical risks. These factories will focus on mature-node chips used in cars and consumer electronics, rather than cutting-edge processors.

China, meanwhile, is accelerating its own semiconductor projects in Indonesia, leveraging its Belt and Road infrastructure to build assembly and testing plants. Beijing sees this as a way to bypass US export controls and maintain access to critical chip-making equipment.

The competition is reshaping Indonesia’s economy, with the government offering tax breaks and land to attract both American and Chinese firms. Local workers are being trained in advanced manufacturing techniques, creating thousands of high-skilled jobs in a region traditionally reliant on natural resources.

However, experts warn that this rapid expansion could lead to technology leaks and intellectual property disputes. Both superpowers are closely guarding their proprietary processes, making it difficult for Indonesian partners to gain full access to cutting-edge know-how.

Ultimately, Southeast Asia is becoming a new battleground for semiconductor dominance, with Indonesia emerging as a surprising winner. The island nation must carefully balance these competing interests to avoid becoming a pawn in the larger US-China tech war.
