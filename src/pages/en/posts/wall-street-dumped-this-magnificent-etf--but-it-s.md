Wall Street discarded the Roundhill Magnificent Seven ETF in late March as tariff anxiety and a sudden rotation out of momentum stocks turned the benchmark for Big Tech into a falling knife. The fund, symbol MAGS, had become the poster child for crowded positioning: equal-weighted exposure to Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, Nvidia and Tesla meant any tremor in AI sentiment would hit it with disproportionate force. From its February record to April 9 the ETF slumped more than 25 percent. Hedge funds, which had used it as a proxy for the entire artificial intelligence trade, liquidated at speed. The narrative was that the AI bubble was popping, Chinese models like DeepSeek and Qwen had made expensive Western infrastructure redundant, and Nvidia’s exceptionalism was over.

The market was wrong. Since April 10 the MAGS ETF has surged 40 percent, staging a roaring comeback that has left sidelined institutional money scrambling to cover. The catalyst is not a single event but a decisive repricing of Nvidia’s role in global compute demand. The very Chinese breakthroughs that spooked the Street in January are now being interpreted correctly: open-source reasoning models from DeepSeek and Alibaba’s Qwen do not kill appetite for GPU clusters, they multiply it. Enterprises and cloud providers in China, South Korea and the Middle East are racing to deploy these models, and that means more orders for H200 and Blackwell systems. Nvidia’s data-centre revenue guidance, confirmed during its May earnings call, has now been revised upward by the supply chain — TSMC’s CoWoS-L capacity is booked into mid-2026.

The comeback of the Magnificent ETF is a repudiation of the shallow narrative that cheap models would crush hardware demand. If anything, Jevons paradox is playing out in real time: the more efficient inference becomes, the broader the adoption, and the larger the total need for heigh-performance silicon. Nvidia, fresh off its Blackwell Ultra reveal at Computex, is not only holding pricing power but accelerating the cadence of its architecture, a signal that the hyperscaler capex cycle has years to run. Against this backdrop, a levered or equal-weight basket of giant-cap AI enablers stops looking like a crowded relic and starts looking like an under-owned reopening bet.

The macro climate has shifted in the ETF’s favour as well. The Federal Reserve’s pause, coupled with Treasury Secretary Bessent’s guidance that the tariff agenda will not immediately hit the semiconductor exemption, removed the most acute tail risk. Meanwhile, the dollar’s softening has made dollar-denominated US tech assets more attractive to foreign institutions, which had been absent during the sell-off. Flows into MAGS in the past six weeks have hit levels not seen since the ETF’s inception, according to Bloomberg, with notable buying from Singaporean sovereign funds and European pension money.

China’s domestic AI models deserve credit for altering the competitive landscape, but they are feeding the same ecosystem they were supposed to disrupt. Kimi’s latest multimodal agent, which matches GPT-4o in key benchmarks, runs on Nvidia GPUs inside Chinese data centres and is being rolled out by Alibaba Cloud. Qwen 2.5 variants are now embedded in DingTalk and enterprise workflows, each deployment adding to GPU consumption. The notion that China would sever its dependence on Nvidia via homegrown chips has proved premature; Huawei’s Ascend series is gaining share, but the performance gap for large-scale training remains wide enough to keep Nvidia’s Hopper and Blackwell lines indispensable.

Tesla, another heavyweight in MAGS, has contributed to the ETF’s rally with its renewed full-self-driving optimism, but the core story is AI infrastructure. Meta’s decision to raise capex guidance again, Microsoft’s Azure growth acceleration and Amazon’s custom silicon push all reinforce the same conclusion: the magnificent seven are not a monolith, but they are united by a structural investment cycle that the market briefly mispriced. The 40 percent jump since April 10 reflects not a speculative bounce but a correction of that mispricing.

For investors who assumed the AI trade had migrated exclusively to private markets or second-order plays such as power and cooling, the MAGS surge is a reminder that the largest public companies remain the most direct beneficiaries of AI spending. The ETF’s comeback says less about Wall Street’s mood swings and more about the durability of the semiconductor backbone. Nvidia, TSMC and the supply chain behind them are not being commoditised; they are being galvanised. That is the truth the April dump obscured and the May recovery has laid bare.
