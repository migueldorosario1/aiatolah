The quantum computing pioneer IonQ burst onto the public markets in October 2021 through a merger with dMY Technology Group III, a special purpose acquisition company. Enthusiasm for the technology was sky-high: IonQ promised to harness trapped-ion qubits to unlock computational capabilities far beyond classical systems. An investor who placed $1,000 in the stock at the SPAC reference price of $10 per share would have acquired 100 shares. The bet was speculative, a wager on a future where quantum computers would reshape drug discovery, materials science and artificial intelligence.

The journey since has been anything but linear. In its first months, IonQ rode a wave of retail and institutional curiosity, spiking above $30 before retreating. The stock endured the brutal selloff in speculative growth names throughout 2022, touching lows near $3. For the original investor, the position would have been decimated, worth only a few hundred dollars. Patient holders, however, were rewarded by a renaissance in quantum computing interest sparked by breakthroughs in error correction, government funding initiatives, and the growing recognition that quantum could become an essential complement to classical AI hardware. By late 2024, IonQ shares soared past $40 as the narrative around quantum’s imminent utility gained traction, briefly turning that $1,000 stake into more than $4,000.

As of early 2025, following some cooling of speculative fervor, IonQ trades around $25 per share. The initial $1,000 investment is now worth approximately $2,500—a 150 percent gain over roughly three and a half years. On an annualized basis, that clocks in at nearly 30 percent, a performance many fund managers would envy. Yet the path to those returns was laced with gut-wrenching drawdowns and required conviction in a technology that still sits at the frontier between laboratory promise and commercial reality.

The comparison with Nvidia, the category giant, is instructive. In October 2021, Nvidia shares were adjusting to a four-for-one stock split, trading at roughly $50 on a post-split basis. A $1,000 bet would have secured 20 shares. Today, Nvidia rides at around $130, propelled by its dominance in AI training semiconductors. That stake would be worth $2,600, a 160 percent return—nearly identical to IonQ’s outcome over the same window. The difference, of course, lies in volatility and fundamental underpinnings. Nvidia multiplied its revenue and earnings many times over, while IonQ’s financials remain nascent, with revenues measured in tens of millions and no clear path to profitability. The returns from IonQ were entirely driven by multiple expansion and shifts in narrative, while Nvidia’s were rooted in staggering operational execution.

For investors, the lesson is double-edged. Frontier technologies can deliver outsized returns, but they demand an iron stomach and a clear-eyed view of the timeline. IonQ’s ride exemplifies the quantum sector’s profile: asymmetric upside punctuated by steep corrections, with catalysts that can take years to materialize. Meanwhile, Nvidia’s steadier climb—despite its own bouts of volatility—demonstrates the power of investing behind a proven ecosystem already reshaping the global economy. Both delivered similar capital gains, but the risk profiles were worlds apart. As quantum computing inches closer to practical advantage, the gap between narrative and fundamental value will narrow, but until then, bets on names like IonQ remain a high-stakes lottery on scientific progress, with the ever-present risk of being just as worthless as they are visionary.
