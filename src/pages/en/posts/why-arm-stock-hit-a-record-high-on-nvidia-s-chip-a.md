Nvidia’s Latest Chip Reveal Proves Arm Is the Quiet Kingmaker of the AI Era

When Nvidia lifts the curtain on a new chip, markets have learned to expect a ripple — but rarely does that ripple lift another company’s stock to an all-time high. Yet that is exactly what happened with Arm Holdings, whose shares surged to a record after Nvidia’s latest silicon announcement. The jump wasn’t about phones or tablets, Arm’s traditional stronghold. It was a crystal-clear signal that Arm has cemented itself as the architecture of choice for the hyperscale data center, and that Nvidia’s AI hegemony is dragging Arm along with it.

Nvidia’s new generation of AI superchips, built around the Grace CPU and Blackwell GPU architecture, incorporates a high number of powerful Arm Neoverse cores to manage data pipelines, security, and orchestration. In one widely watched keynote, Jensen Huang explicitly framed the Grace Arm-based processor as the “engine that feeds the GPU beast.” That line alone was a multibillion-dollar endorsement. By putting Arm at the heart of its datacenter platform — including the GB200 Grace Blackwell Superchip, which pairs two Blackwell GPUs with an Arm-based Grace CPU — Nvidia has sent an unequivocal message to the market: the future of AI computing is not just a GPU story, but a CPU-plus-GPU story, and the CPU side runs on Arm.

For Arm, the financial implications are massive. Unlike its legacy mobile business, where royalties are measured in cents per chip, the data center market commands far higher per-chip royalties and licensing fees. Arm’s v9 architecture, which Nvidia and other hyperscalers are adopting, also carries a higher royalty rate than previous generations. When Nvidia ships Grace chips by the tens of thousands into data centers for cloud giants like Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, Arm collects a substantially fatter check per socket. The volume is already staggering: Nvidia’s latest Blackwell platform has been described by Huang as the company’s “most ambitious project,” with orders extending well into 2025.

Equally important, Nvidia’s deepening reliance on Arm dispels any residual doubt that Arm is a fringe player in the data center. Since Nvidia’s failed $40 billion bid to acquire Arm in 2022, the relationship between the two companies could have turned awkward. Instead, Nvidia became one of Arm’s most prominent customers and a loud evangelist. By showcasing Grace as a native AI engine — not just a bolt-on — Nvidia is pushing its entire ecosystem, from DGX systems to HGX boards, toward an Arm-compatible future. That gravitational pull forces software developers, OEMs, and competitors to optimize for Arm, widening its moat.

The stock market’s reaction was swift because it connected dots that had been visible but not fully priced in. Arm’s licensing revenue growth had already been accelerating, but Nvidia’s announcement gave concrete form to the “data center multiplier” narrative. Suddenly, it’s not just about smartphones at saturation; it’s about AI factories that will run for a decade on Arm-based CPUs. Nvidia’s own forecast of $100 billion in annual data center revenue by 2027 implies a corresponding surge in the need for Arm cores, turning Arm into a secondary beneficiary of every GPU sold.

Investors also read the nuance that Nvidia’s chip design choices lock in Arm for the long haul. Even if Nvidia eventually designs custom Arm cores in-house, as Apple does, it still must pay Arm architecture licensing fees. There is no getting around it: the ISA is the toll bridge, and Nvidia is sending an ever-increasing convoy of trucks across it. For Arm’s business model, that’s the best kind of customer to have.

The record high, then, is not a momentary spike. It’s a reappraisal of Arm’s addressable market, driven home by the most valuable semiconductor company on Earth placing a very public and very large bet. In the quiet architecture wars underpinning the AI boom, Arm just won a decisive battle — and Wall Street is finally paying attention.
