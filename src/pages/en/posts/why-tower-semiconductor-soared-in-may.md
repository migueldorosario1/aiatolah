Why Tower Semiconductor Soared in May: The Nvidia Connection

Tower Semiconductor delivered one of its strongest monthly performances in years this May, surging more than 30% and decisively breaking out of a prolonged trading range. The Israel-based specialty foundry, which had largely been ignored during the early innings of the artificial intelligence rally, suddenly found itself in the spotlight. The explanation is both simple and revealing: the market finally connected Tower’s obscure capabilities to the insatiable infrastructure demands of Nvidia’s AI ecosystem.

To understand the move, it is essential to look past surface-level narratives. Tower is not a direct supplier of leading-edge logic chips for Nvidia’s GPUs. That crown belongs to TSMC. Instead, Tower dominates a less glamorous but equally critical layer of the AI hardware stack: high-performance analog, radio frequency, and silicon photonics components. These are the technologies that enable the blindingly fast interconnects inside Nvidia’s HGX and DGX systems, the optical networking that stitches together thousands of GPUs, and the advanced power management that keeps monstrous data center racks from melting down. In the AI gold rush, Tower provides the picks, shovels, and plumbing that the hyperscalers cannot do without.

May was the month this reality broke through. Nvidia’s blockbuster fiscal first-quarter earnings on May 22 acted as a catalyst, sending its market value soaring and reigniting a fierce rotation into any name tangentially tied to AI infrastructure. But Tower’s surge began even earlier, driven by a flurry of analyst notes and trade press reports highlighting the foundry’s unique position in silicon photonics. Industry watchers pointed to accelerated adoption of 800G and 1.6T optical transceivers in data centers, a trend that directly benefits Tower’s high-volume silicon germanium (SiGe) and silicon photonics process platforms. With Nvidia’s Blackwell platform demanding unprecedented networking bandwidth, the need for power-efficient, high-speed connectivity has never been more acute. Tower’s long-standing partnerships with optical module leaders place it squarely in the supply chain of the very switches and transceivers that make Nvidia’s GPU clusters possible.

Beyond optics, Tower’s capability in power management ICs built on 65nm and 300mm wafers is another linchpin. AI servers consume staggering amounts of electricity; sophisticated voltage regulation and power delivery components are not an afterthought but a design imperative. As Nvidia’s system designs grow ever more complex, the bill of materials for ancillary chips swells. Tower’s factories in Israel and Japan produce these vital analog parts at scale, and the company has been methodically expanding capacity to meet soaring demand. The market rewarded that positioning in May as investors began to discount a multi-year upcycle in AI-driven analog and mixed-signal foundry services.

It would be a mistake to dismiss Tower’s rise as mere meme-stock froth or indiscriminate AI exuberance. Unlike some pre-revenue startups, Tower is deeply profitable, with a diversified customer base spanning industrial, automotive, and medical markets in addition to data center. The May rally reflects a genuine reassessment of earnings power. The Nvidia connection is not a speculative fantasy; it is a physical reality that shows up in lead times, wafer orders, and utilization rates. While TSMC captures the headlines for churning out the world’s most advanced logic, Tower Semiconductor is quietly fabricating the connective tissue that allows those logic chips to function as a coherent system. The market’s awakening to that role was long overdue, and May’s performance suggests the repricing has only just begun.
